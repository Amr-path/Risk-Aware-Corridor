\title{Risk-Aware Corridor-Constrained Pathfinding for UAV Navigation in Biosecurity-Sensitive Environments}

\author[usm]{Amr Elshahed\corref{cor1}}
\ead{amr.elshahed@student.usm.my}

\author[usm]{Majid Khan Bin Majahar Ali}
\author[usm]{Farah Aini Binti Abdullah}

\address[usm]{School of Computer Sciences, Universiti Sains Malaysia, 11800 Penang, Malaysia}

\cortext[cor1]{Corresponding author.}

\begin{abstract}
Unmanned aerial vehicles operating over container terminals and similar transport facilities need routes that are cheap to recompute yet respect spatially varying operational risk, but classical grid planners scale poorly once a risk term enters the cost function and obstacles change between queries. We study corridor-constrained search, which restricts A* to a spatial band around the start--goal line and widens it only when no path fits. Three variants are evaluated on risk-annotated grids: Incremental Line Search (ILS, fixed width, prior work) and two introduced here that adapt the width---Adaptive ILS (AILS) to local obstacle density and Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS) to the local risk field. Edge cost is Euclidean distance plus a weighted risk term. We cast corridor search as shortest-path over a nested chain of induced subgraphs and prove admissibility, termination, completeness, a per-query sub-optimality certificate, corridor-size and time/space bounds, the unsoundness of Jump Point Search once vertices carry weight, and the non-existence of any instance-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ guarantee on weighted grids. Across sixteen experiments and more than $6{,}000$ planned paths---procedural grids, a real container terminal (Penang Port) rasterised from OpenStreetMap, Moving AI Lab benchmarks, and comparisons with Jump Point Search, D*~Lite and Weighted A*---ILS attains up to $7.90\times$ speedup with $86\%$ fewer node expansions while staying within $1.3\%$ of optimal, AILS reaches $3.4\times$ on the Penang terminal, and RILS holds near-exact optimality. The $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ node reduction holds invariant from $200\times200$ to $2000\times2000$ grids. The result is a parameter-light acceleration layer with a clear applicability envelope rather than a universal speedup.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% ====================================================================

Autonomous unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) now fly biosecurity-sensitive missions: contamination surveillance over port facilities, agricultural pest monitoring, quarantine-zone inspection~\citep{WHO2021,FAO2023}. Two demands pull against each other in these settings. The planner has to be fast enough to re-plan in real time, and it has to reason about spatially varying risk, because a route through a contamination gradient, across a quarantine boundary, or into an active inspection area can be operationally unacceptable even when it is the shortest one on the map.

Classical grid planners --- A*, Dijkstra and their relatives --- return optimal paths but pay for it with state-space growth that is quadratic in the grid dimension, and risk weighting makes this worse rather than better. Once each cell carries a continuous risk value, the cost becomes $\mathrm{cost} = \mathrm{dist} + \lambda \cdot \mathrm{risk}(cell)$ for a user-chosen weight~$\lambda$, the planner has to evaluate that composite cost at every expansion, and the optimal path is pulled away from the straight line, so the search fans out further than it would on a plain occupancy grid.

The standard accelerators each solve part of the problem and miss the rest. Jump Point Search~\citep{Harabor2011} prunes symmetric expansions for order-of-magnitude gains, but only on uniform-cost grids. Hierarchical schemes like HPA*~\citep{Botea2004} and Contraction Hierarchies~\citep{Geisberger2008} answer queries quickly once an expensive preprocessing step is paid, but that step assumes the map holds still. Incremental planners such as D*~Lite~\citep{Koenig2002} and LPA*~\citep{Koenig2004} repair a stale search tree cheaply when a handful of cells change, but bog down when the changes are large and clustered --- which is exactly what a newly flagged contamination zone or a redrawn quarantine boundary looks like, a contiguous block rather than a scatter of single cells. None of them covers all three of fast re-planning, risk weighting, and tolerance of clustered change at once.

This paper studies corridor-constrained pathfinding as a way to close that gap.
The core idea, formalised as Incremental Line Search (ILS)~\citep{Elshahed2025ILS} and extended here with the density-adaptive variant Adaptive ILS (AILS) and the risk-responsive variant Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS), restricts the search space to a spatial corridor centred on the Bresenham line between start and goal and expands the corridor incrementally only when the initial search fails to find a path.
Prior work on ILS demonstrated up to $87.31\%$ execution-time reduction on standard occupancy grids~\citep{Elshahed2025ILS}; this paper presents AILS and RILS in self-contained form (Section~\ref{sec:method}) and reports their performance alongside ILS on risk-weighted grids (e.g., Experiment~9: $66.8\%$ mean node reduction for AILS at $500 \times 500$ resolution).
The application to risk-weighted grids has not been investigated. The corridor's interaction with the risk landscape produces qualitatively different optimality and efficiency tradeoffs from those seen in the uniform-cost case.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The contributions of this paper are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
\item We evaluate ILS on risk-annotated grids across three risk distributions, six risk-weight values, and three obstacle densities. ILS reaches speedups of up to $7.90\times$ with $86\%$ node reduction, and path quality stays within $0.2\%$ of optimal for gradient and uniform risk at $\lambda \leq 1.0$.

\item We introduce Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS), a new variant that adapts corridor width to the local risk level. RILS attains the best path quality among the three corridor methods (under $0.1\%$ suboptimal on hotspot risk, against $0.7$ to $1.2\%$ for ILS and AILS at $\lambda \leq 1.0$) but its speedup is lower. The result clarifies the tradeoff between risk-adaptive corridor construction and search-space restriction, and RILS is therefore the preferred variant when path quality dominates the cost function.

\item We provide a graph-theoretic analysis of corridor-restricted search as optimisation over nested induced subgraphs. It establishes heuristic admissibility, termination, containment and completeness, a per-query a-posteriori sub-optimality certificate (a cheap $O(1)$ bound and an exact bound costing one unrestricted search), a corridor-size bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (4r+1)(D_\infty+2r+1)$ with a $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ node-expansion reduction, and time/space complexity for ILS/AILS/RILS. We prove that no instance-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ bound exists for corridor-restricted search on weighted grids, and that Jump Point Search is unsound on vertex-weighted grids, which formally delimits where symmetry pruning and corridor restriction each apply.

\item We compare ILS and AILS against D*~Lite~\citep{Koenig2002} and Weighted A*~\citep{Pohl1970} and show under which operating conditions each method is preferable.

\item On procedurally generated container-port grids with structured obstacle layouts and biosecurity risk layers, ILS scales from $1.0\times$ to $3.9\times$ speedup with grid size. AILS shows a threshold effect, in that its adaptive-corridor overhead exceeds the benefits on grids below $500 \times 500$.

\item In a progressive obstacle discovery simulation over 180 sustained UAV missions, corridor-constrained re-planning achieves higher mission success rates than standard A* (up to $73.3\%$ against $60.0\%$) and $12.6\%$ cumulative node reduction. The cost is somewhat higher path suboptimality.

\item We validate the methods on a real container terminal (Penang North Butterworth Container Terminal) rasterised from public OpenStreetMap data, demonstrating that the speedup--quality tradeoff transfers from procedurally generated grids to a real-world port topology, and providing a publicly reproducible benchmark.
\end{enumerate}

Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on grid-based pathfinding, risk-aware planning, and search-space restriction techniques.
Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the ILS, AILS, and RILS algorithms adapted for risk-weighted grids, along with formal sub-optimality analysis.

Section~\ref{sec:results} reports and analyses the results.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} synthesises the findings and discusses implications for biosecurity UAV operations.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Classical Grid-Based Pathfinding}

Grid-based pathfinding has been studied extensively in robotics and artificial intelligence.
Dijkstra's algorithm~\citep{Dijkstra1959} finds shortest paths on weighted graphs but explores the entire reachable space.
A*~\citep{Hart1968} reduces exploration through heuristic guidance, achieving optimality with admissible heuristics.
Bidirectional search variants reduce average-case complexity by searching from both endpoints~\citep{Goldberg2005}.
These algorithms form the baseline against which acceleration techniques are measured.

\subsection{Symmetry-Breaking and Jump Point Search}

Jump Point Search~\citep{Harabor2011} achieves dramatic speedups by identifying and pruning symmetric path expansions on uniform-cost grids.
JPS+ with preprocessing~\citep{Harabor2014} further accelerates queries through precomputed jump point maps.
Subgoal graphs~\citep{Uras2013} and rectangular symmetry reduction~\citep{Harabor2014RSR} offer alternative symmetry-exploitation strategies.
A fundamental limitation shared by all symmetry-based methods is their restriction to uniform-cost grids: the pruning rules that identify symmetric paths rely on the assumption that all traversable cells have identical cost, which breaks down on risk-annotated grids where cell costs vary spatially.

\subsection{Hierarchical and Preprocessing-Based Methods}

HPA*~\citep{Botea2004} decomposes the grid into clusters with precomputed inter-cluster paths, enabling fast query-time planning at the cost of offline preprocessing.
Contraction Hierarchies~\citep{Geisberger2008} contract the graph by adding shortcut edges, achieving near-constant query time on road networks.
Navigation meshes~\citep{Tozour2002} represent free space as convex polygons for efficient any-angle pathfinding, while Theta*~\citep{Nash2007} enables any-angle paths on grids through line-of-sight checks.
These methods require stable environments; dynamic obstacle changes invalidate the preprocessing and necessitate costly updates.

\subsection{Incremental and Dynamic Re-planning}

D*~Lite~\citep{Koenig2002} and LPA*~\citep{Koenig2004} maintain a search tree that can be repaired incrementally when the map changes between queries.
Field D*~\citep{Ferguson2005} extends this to continuous-cost interpolation on grids.
These planners are optimal for small, frequent perturbations (e.g., a robot discovering single blocked cells as it moves) but suffer when changes are large and clustered, because the priority queue maintenance propagates updates through much of the search space.

\subsection{Risk-Aware and Exposure-Sensitive Planning}

Risk-aware pathfinding incorporates non-distance costs into the planning objective.
Composite cost functions of the form $\mathrm{cost} = \mathrm{dist} + \lambda \cdot \mathrm{risk}$ have been applied in military route planning~\citep{Rowe1990}, hazardous materials transport~\citep{Erkut1998}, and UAV mission planning~\citep{Aggarwal2020}; the classical weighted-region problem of \citet{Mitchell1991} is the continuous-space root of this lineage.
Recent UAV work makes the risk field explicit: \citet{Primatesta2019} plan with a population-risk map using a riskA* variant, \citet{Tang2023} minimise a third-party urban risk field with a min-cost A*, and \citet{Karlsson2023} give the risk-aware incremental planner D*+ for heterogeneous platforms; learning-accelerated risk-field A* has also been explored~\citep{Xiang2024}. These methods all reshape the cost landscape but search the full grid; the corridor mechanism studied here is orthogonal and could wrap any of them.
The challenge is that risk weighting reshapes the cost landscape, potentially routing optimal paths far from the geometric shortest path, which increases computational cost.
Alternative approaches include bounded-suboptimal methods that trade optimality for speed through heuristic inflation, as discussed in Section \ref{subsec:bounded}, or sampling-based and learning-based techniques reviewed in Sections \ref{subsec:sampling} and \ref{subsec:uav_learning}.
Corridor-based restriction offers a complementary approach: by confining search to a spatial band around the expected path direction, it limits the additional nodes that risk weighting forces into consideration.

\subsection{Bounded-Suboptimal Search}\label{subsec:bounded}

Bounded-suboptimal search methods trade optimality for computational speed by systematically relaxing the optimality requirement.
Weighted A* (wA*)~\citep{Pohl1970} multiplies the heuristic by a weight factor $w > 1$, yielding solutions guaranteed to be within a factor of $w$ of optimal (i.e., $w$-bounded suboptimal), typically with dramatic speedup: $f = g + w \cdot h$.
Anytime Weighted A*~\citep{Hansen2007} extends this by running a sequence of searches with decreasing weight values, returning progressively better solutions as time permits.
Potential Search~\citep{Stern2014}, already cited above for its foundational work on bounded-cost corridors, uses a potential function to estimate path cost and prunes nodes that provably cannot lead to solutions within a user-specified cost bound.
Focal Search~\citep{Pearl1982} maintains two open lists and prioritizes nodes in a ``focal'' set within a bounded distance of the best-known solution cost.
These methods differ from corridor-based methods in the underlying mechanism. Bounded-suboptimal approaches trade optimality for speed through heuristic inflation, that is, by manipulating the evaluation function. Corridor-based methods achieve the speed-versus-quality tradeoff through spatial restriction, by limiting the search space itself.
The two mechanisms have different applicability profiles. Heuristic inflation can degrade solution quality in risk-sensitive environments where cost inflation is unacceptable; spatial restriction maintains optimality within the constrained region.

\subsection{Sampling-Based and Multi-Objective Planning}\label{subsec:sampling}

Sampling-based methods sidestep explicit grid discretization by randomly sampling the configuration space and constructing roadmaps or trees.
RRT* (Rapidly-exploring Random Tree Star)~\citep{Karaman2011} probabilistically achieves asymptotic optimality by iteratively expanding a tree with cost-based rewiring, enabling handling of complex cost functions and continuous configuration spaces.
PRM* and its variants~\citep{Karaman2011} extend Probabilistic Roadmaps for multi-query planning with optimality guarantees.
However, these methods lack completeness guarantees on discrete grids and require significant computational resources for convergence to near-optimal solutions in high-dimensional spaces.
Multi-objective path planning methods extend classical pathfinding to scenarios with multiple competing objectives.
NAMOA* (Namoa* for multi-objective A*)~\citep{Mandow2010} computes the set of Pareto-optimal paths, enabling decision-makers to balance multiple criteria (e.g., distance vs.\ risk vs.\ time).
While powerful for complex decision problems, these methods incur additional computational overhead compared to single-objective methods.
Sampling-based and multi-objective methods are most effective in unstructured or continuous environments with complex cost functions. For the structured grid environments typical of UAV port operations, classical grid-based acceleration techniques such as symmetry exploitation and spatial restriction give better computational efficiency without sacrificing optimality guarantees.

\subsection{Recent UAV Path Planning and Learning-Based Approaches}\label{subsec:uav_learning}

Recent advances in autonomous systems have explored learning-based approaches for UAV path planning.
Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) methods train neural networks to map observations to actions, potentially discovering novel planning strategies~\citep{Jones2023}.
Hybrid approaches combining classical planning algorithms with machine learning (e.g., learning heuristics or using DRL for configuration selection) have shown promise in adapting to dynamic environments~\citep{Jones2023,Aggarwal2020}.
For biosecurity-critical operations, learning-based methods have important limitations. They require substantial training data specific to the operational environment. They lack formal optimality or safety guarantees. And they cannot easily encode hard constraints such as ``avoid pathogen release zones''.
Classical grid-based methods with explicit acceleration techniques remain the preferred choice when solution quality, completeness, and verifiability are required.

\subsection{Corridor and Geometric Search-Space Restriction}\label{subsec:geometric_restriction}

Restricting search by geometry, rather than by an evaluation-function bound, is a recurring idea, and we position the present work explicitly within it.
\emph{Preprocessing-based geometric pruning} stores, for each edge, a region that contains all goals reachable optimally through that edge, and prunes any expansion whose goal falls outside the region: geometric containers~\citep{Wagner2005} and arc-flags for road networks, and the grid-specific goal bounding-box pruning of \citet{Rabin2016}, which combines per-edge bounding boxes with JPS+ for state-of-the-art uniform-cost grid queries. These methods are powerful but require all-pairs (or all-edges) preprocessing and assume a static graph, so obstacle changes invalidate the precomputation.
\emph{Frontier and cost pruning}\,---\,beam search~\citep{Bisiani1987} (fixed-size frontier) and bounded-cost / potential search~\citep{Stern2014} (prune by a cost bound)\,---\,restrict the search without a spatial region.
\emph{Trajectory corridors} such as the safe flight corridors of \citet{Liu2017} construct a convex free-space region \emph{around an already-found path} to host a trajectory-optimisation program; here the corridor is an output container for smoothing, not an input restriction on graph search. Closest in mechanism and reported effect is the corridor-constrained Hybrid~A* of \citet{Che2026}, which confines a kinodynamic search to a Voronoi-derived safe corridor and reports an $83.7\%$ reduction in node expansions\,---\,but on car-like, uniform-cost motion, with the corridor built from a reference path and no incremental widening or completeness guarantee.

Against this backdrop, \citet{Elshahed2025ILS} formalised Incremental Line Search (ILS), which builds a corridor directly along the Bresenham line between start and goal\,---\,requiring \emph{no preprocessing}\,---\,and widens it incrementally only on failure, evaluated on uniform-cost occupancy grids.
The contribution of the present paper is therefore not the corridor idea in the abstract but a specific, defensible combination: (i) a preprocessing-free, incrementally-widened line corridor applied to \emph{risk-weighted} (vertex-weighted) grids, where the symmetry-based pruning of \citet{Rabin2016,Harabor2011} is unsound (Lemma~\ref{lem:jps_unsound}); (ii) a \emph{risk-field-adaptive} corridor width (RILS) computed in $O(1)$ per reference cell via summed-area tables; and (iii) a formal package\,---\,induced-subgraph framing, containment/termination/completeness, a per-query sub-optimality certificate, corridor-size and complexity bounds, and a proof that no instance-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ bound exists on weighted grids\,---\,that prior corridor work does not provide.
Concretely, we also present the density-adaptive variant AILS, which uses integral images for $O(1)$ local density queries (Section~\ref{sec:method}), and the risk-responsive variant RILS (Section~\ref{subsec:rils}); all three are validated on risk-weighted grids in biosecurity-specific environments.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

We consider pathfinding on a 2D grid graph $G = (V, E, w)$ where $V$ is the set of traversable cells, $E$ connects 8-adjacent free cells, and $w: E \to \mathbb{R}^+$ assigns edge weights.
Each cell $v \in V$ has an optional risk value $\rho(v) \in [0, 1]$; we write $\rho_{\max} = \max_{v \in V} \rho(v) \leq 1$.
The cost of traversing an edge $(u, v) \in E$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cost}
    w(u, v) = d(u, v) + \lambda \cdot \rho(v)
\end{equation}
where $d(u, v)$ is the Euclidean movement cost ($1.0$ for cardinal, $\sqrt{2}$ for diagonal moves) and $\lambda \geq 0$ is the risk-weight parameter.
At $\lambda = 0$ the problem reduces to standard shortest-path planning; increasing $\lambda$ progressively penalises high-risk cells.

The objective is to find a path $\pi^* = (v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_k)$ from start $s = v_0$ to goal $g = v_k$ that minimises:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:objective}
    C(\pi) = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} w(v_i, v_{i+1})
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Corridor as an induced subgraph.}
Let $\ell(s,g)$ be the Bresenham digital segment between $s$ and $g$ (a fixed tie-breaking convention gives one cell per step along the dominant axis, an $8$-connected lattice path of $\|s-g\|_\infty + 1$ cells that is monotone and $1$-Lipschitz in the minor coordinate). For half-width $r \geq 0$ the corridor vertex set is $\mathcal{C}(r) = \{v \in V : d_\infty(v, \ell(s,g)) \leq r\}$, where $d_\infty$ is the Chebyshev distance to the line set, and $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)} := G[\mathcal{C}(r)]$ is the subgraph of $G$ \emph{induced} by $\mathcal{C}(r)$ (an edge is retained iff both endpoints lie in $\mathcal{C}(r)$). With $r_k = r_0 + (k-1)\Delta r$ the induced subgraphs are nested, $G[\mathcal{C}(r_1)] \subseteq G[\mathcal{C}(r_2)] \subseteq \cdots \subseteq G$, and ILS runs an optimal base search on the first member that admits an $s$-to-$g$ path. AILS and RILS replace the constant $r$ by a per-reference-cell radius $r(p)$, yielding a variable-width induced subgraph. This induced-subgraph (vertex-set) restriction is the unifying object of the analysis in Section~\ref{subsec:formal}; it is what distinguishes corridor methods from heuristic-inflation (which alters the evaluation function) and from symmetry pruning (which is sound only on uniform-cost grids, Lemma~\ref{lem:jps_unsound}).

\subsection{Incremental Line Search (ILS)}

ILS~\citep{Elshahed2025ILS} constrains A* search to a spatial corridor $\mathcal{C}$ centred on the Bresenham line $\ell(s, g)$ between start and goal.
The corridor is constructed as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:corridor}
    \mathcal{C}(r) = \{v \in V : \min_{p \in \ell(s,g)} \|v - p\|_\infty \leq r\}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the corridor half-width.
A* search is then restricted to $\mathcal{C}$: only cells within the corridor are expanded.
If the search fails (no path exists within $\mathcal{C}$), the corridor width is increased by $\Delta r$ and the search is re-run.
This expansion continues for up to $K_{\max}$ attempts.

The initial corridor width is set as a fraction $\alpha_0$ of the grid diagonal: $r_0 = \alpha_0 \cdot \sqrt{H^2 + W^2}$, where $H$ and $W$ are the grid dimensions.
Empirically, $\alpha_0 = 0.05$ (5\% of diagonal) provides a good balance between corridor tightness and path feasibility.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{ILS: Incremental Line Search on Risk-Weighted Grid}
\label{alg:ils}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Grid $G$, start $s$, goal $g$, risk weight $\lambda$, initial width $r_0$, max attempts $K_{\max}$
\ENSURE Path $\pi^*$ or FAILURE
\STATE $r \leftarrow r_0$
\FOR{$k = 1$ \TO $K_{\max}$}
    \STATE Build corridor $\mathcal{C}(r)$ via Bresenham line $\ell(s, g)$
    \STATE $\pi \leftarrow$ A*$(G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}, s, g, \lambda)$ \COMMENT{Search restricted to $\mathcal{C}$}
    \IF{$\pi \neq$ NULL}
        \RETURN $\pi$
    \ENDIF
    \STATE $r \leftarrow r + \Delta r$ \COMMENT{Expand corridor}
\ENDFOR
\RETURN FAILURE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Adaptive ILS (AILS)}

AILS replaces the uniform-width corridor of ILS with a variable-width corridor whose radius at each reference-line cell adapts to local obstacle density.
The key mechanism is the integral image (summed area table) $I$, which enables $O(1)$ density queries:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density}
    \delta(p, \omega) = \frac{1}{(2\omega+1)^2} \sum_{(r,c) \in W(p,\omega)} \mathbf{1}[\text{obstacle at } (r,c)]
\end{equation}
where $W(p, \omega)$ is the $(2\omega+1) \times (2\omega+1)$ window centred at point $p$ on the reference line.

The corridor radius at each reference point is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adaptive_radius}
    r(p) = r_{\min} + (r_{\max} - r_{\min}) \cdot \delta(p, \omega)^{\gamma}
\end{equation}
where $r_{\min}$ and $r_{\max}$ bound the corridor width, and $\gamma$ (the AILS density exponent, distinct from the corridor fraction $\alpha$) controls sensitivity to density changes.
High local density produces a wider corridor (to accommodate detours around obstacles); low density produces a narrow corridor (to maximise search-space exclusion). AILS otherwise follows the ILS loop of Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils}, replacing the constant radius $r$ with the per-cell radius $r(p)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:adaptive_radius} computed from the obstacle integral image.

\subsection{Risk-Aware Heuristic Adaptation}

On risk-weighted grids, the standard octile distance heuristic $h(n, g)$ remains admissible for $\lambda = 0$ but underestimates true cost when $\lambda > 0$ because it ignores risk contributions.
We adjust the heuristic as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heuristic}
    h_\lambda(n, g) = h(n, g) \cdot (1 + \lambda \cdot \bar{\rho})
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\rho}$ is a conservative estimate of mean risk along the remaining path (we use $\bar{\rho} = 0.3$ based on empirical grid statistics).
This adjustment maintains admissibility for moderate $\lambda$ values while improving search guidance.

\subsection{Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS)}\label{subsec:rils}

AILS adapts corridor width based on local obstacle density. Its corridor can therefore be too narrow in regions with sparse obstacles but elevated risk, which are exactly the regions where a wider corridor is needed to allow risk-avoidance detours.
We propose Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS), a variant that adapts corridor width to the local risk distribution rather than to obstacle density.

RILS constructs the corridor along the same Bresenham reference line as ILS and AILS, but determines the radius at each reference point $p$ using the local mean risk within a query window:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:risk_query}
    \bar{\rho}(p, \omega) = \frac{1}{(2\omega+1)^2} \sum_{(r,c) \in W(p,\omega)} \rho(r,c)
\end{equation}
where $W(p, \omega)$ is the $(2\omega+1) \times (2\omega+1)$ window centred at $p$ and $\rho(r,c)$ is the risk value at cell $(r,c)$.
This query is computed in $O(1)$ time using an integral image (summed area table) over the risk layer, analogous to AILS's density queries but operating on the continuous risk values rather than binary obstacle indicators.

The corridor radius at each reference point is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rils_radius}
    r_{\text{RILS}}(p) = r_{\text{base}} + (r_{\max} - r_{\text{base}}) \cdot \bar{\rho}(p, \omega)^\beta
\end{equation}
where $r_{\text{base}}$ is the minimum corridor width (the RILS counterpart of AILS's $r_{\min}$), $r_{\max}$ is the maximum width, and $\beta \geq 0$ controls risk-sensitivity.
In high-risk regions ($\bar{\rho} \to 1$), the corridor approaches $r_{\max}$, providing ample space for the search to find risk-avoidance detours.
In low-risk regions ($\bar{\rho} \to 0$), the corridor narrows toward $r_{\text{base}}$, maximising search-space pruning where risk avoidance is unnecessary.
When no risk layer is present ($\lambda = 0$), RILS degenerates to standard ILS.

AILS and RILS differ only in the adaptation signal used to set the corridor radius. AILS uses $r(p) \propto \delta(p)^\alpha$, where $\delta$ is the local obstacle density, so the corridor widens near obstacles to allow detours around blocked cells. RILS uses $r(p) \propto \bar{\rho}(p)^\beta$, where $\bar{\rho}$ is the local mean risk, so the corridor widens in high-risk regions to allow risk-avoidance routing.

RILS is therefore designed for risk-weighted grids in which the cost landscape, rather than the obstacle layout, determines path optimality.
Consider an open, obstacle-free region with high risk: RILS produces a wide corridor (good for finding risk-avoiding paths), whereas AILS produces a narrow corridor (because obstacle density is zero) and its adaptation signal is misleading.

Default parameters used in experiments: $r_{\text{base}} = 0.05 \cdot \text{diag}$, $r_{\max} = 0.15 \cdot \text{diag}$, $\beta = 1.0$, $\omega = 5$, up to 10 expansion attempts. Like AILS, RILS reuses the ILS loop of Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils} with the per-cell radius of Eq.~\eqref{eq:rils_radius} in place of the constant $r$, the only change being that the integral image is built over the risk layer rather than the obstacle grid.

\subsection{Formal Sub-Optimality Analysis}\label{subsec:formal}

We now establish theoretical properties of corridor-constrained search on risk-weighted grids.

\paragraph{Unconditionally admissible heuristic.}
The simplest admissible heuristic for risk-weighted grids uses $\bar{\rho} = 0$, reducing to the standard octile distance:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h0}
    h_0(n, g) = h(n, g)
\end{equation}
Since $h(n,g) \leq d_{\min}(n,g) \leq C(\pi)$ for any path $\pi$, this heuristic is admissible for all $\lambda \geq 0$ and all risk distributions, unconditionally.
However, $h_0$ ignores risk and provides weak guidance when $\lambda > 0$.

\paragraph{Risk-adjusted heuristic (conditional).}
To improve search guidance, we use the risk-adjusted heuristic from Eq.~\eqref{eq:heuristic}, restated here with explicit conditions:

\begin{proposition}[Conditional Heuristic Admissibility]\label{prop:admissible}
Let $h_\lambda(n, g) = h(n, g) \cdot (1 + \lambda \cdot \bar{\rho})$ with $\bar{\rho} \geq 0$, where $h(n,g)$ is the octile distance on an 8-connected grid. Then $h_\lambda$ is admissible at $n$ provided every path $\pi$ from $n$ to $g$ with $k$ edges and mean risk $\bar{\rho}_\pi$ satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adm_condition}
    k \cdot \bar{\rho}_\pi \;\geq\; h(n,g) \cdot \bar{\rho}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
For any path $\pi = (n, v_1, \ldots, g)$ with $k$ edges, the true cost decomposes as
\[
C(\pi) = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \bigl[d(v_i, v_{i+1}) + \lambda \cdot \rho(v_{i+1})\bigr] = D(\pi) + \lambda \cdot R(\pi),
\]
where $D(\pi) \geq h(n,g)$ because the octile distance is the minimum-cost path on the unblocked 8-connected grid, and $R(\pi) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \rho(v_i) = k \cdot \bar{\rho}_\pi$ by definition of the mean risk.
Under condition~(\ref{eq:adm_condition}), $\lambda \cdot R(\pi) \geq \lambda \cdot h(n,g) \cdot \bar{\rho}$, so
\[
C(\pi) \;=\; D(\pi) + \lambda \cdot R(\pi) \;\geq\; h(n,g) + \lambda \cdot h(n,g) \cdot \bar{\rho} \;=\; h_\lambda(n,g).
\]
Therefore $h_\lambda$ is admissible at $n$. $\square$

\paragraph{When the condition holds.}
Condition~(\ref{eq:adm_condition}) is satisfied whenever $k \geq h(n,g)$ and $\bar{\rho}_\pi \geq \bar{\rho}$. The first inequality holds for all paths whose minimum-cost step is the cardinal step of length 1 (then $D(\pi) = k$ and $D(\pi) \geq h$ give $k \geq h$); it can fail on pure-diagonal motion, for which $D(\pi) = k\sqrt{2}$ and therefore $k = D(\pi)/\sqrt{2}$ can be below $h(n,g)$.
On such diagonal-dominant paths the condition tightens to $\bar{\rho}_\pi \geq (h/k)\,\bar{\rho}$, which in the all-diagonal extreme equals $\sqrt{2}\,\bar{\rho}$. When the condition fails, $h_\lambda$ is simply inadmissible at $n$ and A* loses its optimality guarantee.

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
The condition~(\ref{eq:adm_condition}) is sufficient but not necessary for admissibility, and with the default $\bar{\rho} = 0.3$ it is in fact \emph{frequently violated} even on smooth risk: the audit in Experiment~14 (Table~\ref{tab:exp14_rho}) finds that at $\bar{\rho} = 0.3$, $\lambda = 1.0$, the returned cost exceeds the true optimum on essentially every query for gradient and hotspot risk (and on $94\%$ of uniform-risk queries). What makes $\bar{\rho} = 0.3$ usable is not that it preserves admissibility but that the resulting \emph{excess} is small and grows predictably with $\bar{\rho}$ and with the spikiness of the risk field: at $\bar{\rho} = 0.3$ the maximum cost excess is $1.0\%$ on gradient, $2.8\%$ on uniform, and $6.4\%$ on hotspot risk, and at $\bar{\rho} = 0.15$ uniform risk ($\rho \equiv 0.3$) is still genuinely admissible ($0\%$ violations), matching the per-edge bound $\bar{\rho} \leq \rho$. The headline path-quality figures therefore reflect this bounded inadmissibility together with the corridor restriction: with $h_\lambda$, Experiment~1 paths stayed within $0.2\%$ of optimal on gradient and uniform risk and $1.3\%$ on hotspot risk for $\lambda \leq 1.0$ (growing to $11\%$ at $\lambda = 5.0$). These are near-optimal, not provably optimal. Where guaranteed optimality is required, the unconditionally admissible $h_0 = h$ ($\bar{\rho} = 0$, zero violations on all distributions in Table~\ref{tab:exp14_rho}) should be used, at the cost of weaker search guidance.

\begin{proposition}[Corridor Containment and Completeness]\label{prop:complete}
If the optimal path $\pi^*$ lies entirely within corridor $\mathcal{C}(r)$, i.e., $\pi^* \subseteq \mathcal{C}(r)$, then corridor-restricted A* returns $\pi^*$.
Furthermore, the incremental expansion mechanism (up to $K_{\max}$ attempts) guarantees that if a path exists within a corridor of width $r_0 + K_{\max} \cdot \Delta r$, it will be found.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
When $\pi^* \subseteq \mathcal{C}(r)$, the corridor restriction removes only nodes not on $\pi^*$, and A* with an admissible heuristic on the restricted graph $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}$ finds $\pi^*$ because all nodes on $\pi^*$ remain in the search space.
For completeness, each failed attempt increases corridor width by $\Delta r$, so after $K_{\max}$ attempts the corridor covers all cells within $d_\infty$-distance $r_0 + K_{\max} \cdot \Delta r$ of the reference line.
$\square$

\paragraph{Termination.}
The incremental expansion loop terminates after finitely many iterations.

\begin{proposition}[Termination]\label{prop:termination}
For any finite $H \times W$ grid $G$ with start $s$, goal $g$, initial half-width $r_0 \geq 0$, increment $\Delta r \geq 1$, and covering width $r_{\mathrm{cover}} = \max(H, W) - 1$ (distinct from the AILS/RILS width cap $r_{\max}$), Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils} (and likewise the AILS and RILS variants) halts in at most $\lceil (r_{\mathrm{cover}} - r_0)/\Delta r \rceil + 1$ iterations. It returns an $s$-to-$g$ path if one exists in $G$ and returns FAILURE otherwise.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
The half-width updates monotonically as $r \leftarrow r + \Delta r$ on each failed attempt, so it exceeds $r_{\mathrm{cover}}$ after at most $\lceil (r_{\mathrm{cover}} - r_0)/\Delta r \rceil + 1$ iterations. Each restricted search terminates because the base algorithm is complete on the finite subgraph $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}$. Every cell of an $H \times W$ grid lies within $d_\infty$-distance $\max(H,W) - 1$ of either endpoint of the reference line $\ell(s,g)$, hence within that distance of $\ell(s,g)$; so when $r = r_{\mathrm{cover}} = \max(H,W) - 1$ the corridor covers $V$ (the choice $\min(H,W)$ is insufficient on non-square grids whose reference line is short and corner-localised), and the final attempt runs the base algorithm on the unrestricted graph $G$: it returns a path if one exists and FAILURE otherwise. The pathological case $\Delta r = 0$ (a corridor that never widens) is excluded by the hypothesis $\Delta r \geq 1$, which is the natural integer increment on an 8-connected grid with unit spacing. $\square$

\paragraph{When the global optimum is returned.}
Proposition~\ref{prop:complete} guarantees that the returned path is cost-optimal \emph{within the realised corridor}. The global optimum is returned exactly when that corridor contains a globally optimal path, and this conditional property is what the empirical \emph{path cost ratio} measures.

\begin{proposition}[A-posteriori sub-optimality certificate]\label{prop:certificate}
Let the base algorithm be cost-optimal on any finite weighted graph (A* with an admissible, consistent heuristic, or Dijkstra), let $\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}$ be the returned path, and let $\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}$ be the corridor at the returning attempt. Then $\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}$ is globally optimal on $G$ if and only if $\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}$ contains a globally optimal path; in particular this holds whenever $r^{\dagger} = r_{\mathrm{cover}}$ (then $G|_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} = G$). The excess cost equals the in-corridor detour cost $\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} := C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}) - C(\pi^*) \geq 0$, and admits two computable certificates:
\begin{align}
\text{(loose)}\quad 0 \;\leq\; \Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} &\;\leq\; C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}) - \underline{C}(s,g), \label{eq:cert_loose}\\
\text{(exact)}\quad \Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} &\;=\; C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}) - C(\pi^*), \label{eq:cert_exact}
\end{align}
where $\underline{C}(s,g)$ is any admissible lower bound on $C(\pi^*)$ (e.g.\ the octile relaxation, evaluable in $O(1)$). The loose certificate~\eqref{eq:cert_loose} costs $O(1)$ beyond the search already performed; the exact certificate~\eqref{eq:cert_exact} requires one unrestricted shortest-path computation on $G$, i.e.\ $O(|V|\log|V|)$ additional time.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
Optimality of $\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}$ on $G|_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}}$ is inherited from the base algorithm's optimality on any finite weighted graph. Its cost therefore equals the minimum in-corridor cost, which exceeds the global optimum $C(\pi^*)$ by exactly $\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}}$; this difference is zero precisely when a globally optimal path lies inside $\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}$, and trivially when $r^{\dagger} = r_{\mathrm{cover}}$. Inequality~\eqref{eq:cert_loose} follows from $C(\pi^*) \geq \underline{C}(s,g)$; equality~\eqref{eq:cert_exact} follows by computing $C(\pi^*)$ exactly with one unrestricted search. $\square$

\noindent\textit{Remark (heuristic consistency).}
The optimality guarantees above require a base search that is optimal on the weighted graph, i.e.\ A* with an admissible \emph{and consistent} heuristic, or Dijkstra. The risk-adjusted heuristic $h_\lambda = h\,(1+\lambda\bar{\rho})$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:heuristic} scales the octile distance by a constant $\kappa = 1+\lambda\bar{\rho} \geq 1$; using consistency of $h$, a sufficient condition for $h_\lambda$ to be consistent is $(\kappa-1)\,d(u,v) \leq \lambda\rho(v)$ on every edge, i.e.\ $\bar{\rho}\,d(u,v) \leq \rho(v)$, which fails in general. The certificates and optimality statements consequently attach to the unconditionally admissible variant $h_0 = h$ (or to Dijkstra); with $h_\lambda$ they hold up to the conditional-admissibility margin of Proposition~\ref{prop:admissible}.

\begin{corollary}[Additive Sub-Optimality Bound]\label{cor:bound}
Let $\pi_r^*$ be the optimal path within $\mathcal{C}(r)$ and $\pi^*$ the globally optimal path, and let the returning corridor be $\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}$. The additive detour is bounded by the in-corridor path length times the maximum edge weight,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:additive_bound}
\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} \;=\; C(\pi_r^*) - C(\pi^*) \;\leq\; \ell_{\max}(\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}) \cdot w_{\max}(G),
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{\max}(\mathcal{C}^{\dagger})$ is the maximum number of edges on an in-corridor $s$-to-$g$ path and $w_{\max}(G) = \max_{(u,v) \in E} w(u,v) = \sqrt{2} + \lambda\rho_{\max}$ is the maximum edge weight. Dividing by $C(\pi^*) > 0$ gives the multiplicative form $C(\pi_r^*)/C(\pi^*) \leq 1 + \ell_{\max}(\mathcal{C}^{\dagger})\,w_{\max}(G)/C(\pi^*)$. This is a worst-case envelope (it discards the $-C(\pi^*)$ term); the operationally useful per-query bound is the a-posteriori certificate of Proposition~\ref{prop:certificate}, of which this is the distribution-agnostic relaxation.
Empirically, the normalised detour $\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}}/C(\pi^*)$ stayed below $0.002$ for gradient and uniform risk at $\lambda \leq 1.0$ (Table~\ref{tab:exp1_speedup}) and below $0.013$ for hotspot risk at $\lambda \leq 1.0$, far inside this worst-case envelope.
\end{corollary}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
The base algorithm's optimality on $G|_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}}$ (as in Proposition~\ref{prop:certificate}) makes $\pi_r^* = \pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}$ the minimum-cost in-corridor path; it uses at most $\ell_{\max}(\mathcal{C}^{\dagger})$ edges, each of weight at most $w_{\max}(G)$, and $C(\pi^*) \geq 0$. $\square$

The bound establishes that corridor-constrained search is near-optimal when the corridor captures the globally optimal path or a close substitute.
The incremental expansion mechanism serves as a safety net: if the initial corridor is too narrow, successive expansions widen it until a feasible path is found.
The empirical path quality results in Section~\ref{sec:results} confirm that this worst-case bound is loose; actual sub-optimality is substantially smaller.

\paragraph{On tighter bounds.}
The bound~\eqref{eq:additive_bound} is distribution-agnostic and therefore necessarily loose.
Distribution-specific behaviour differs sharply. For uniform risk ($\rho = c$), $\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} = 0$ trivially since all paths incur identical risk per unit distance.
For gradient risk (where $\rho$ varies smoothly), the detour is governed by the local risk-gradient magnitude $\|\nabla\rho\|_{\max}$ and the corridor width, so the cost slack shrinks with a smoother field; deriving a tight constant here is the open conditional-bound question stated below.
Hotspot risk admits no simple tighter bound, because the deviation depends on whether the optimal path's detour around a hotspot falls inside or outside the corridor, which is a configuration-dependent property.

The natural question is whether a corridor method can guarantee an \emph{instance-independent} multiplicative bound, that is, a single constant $(1+\epsilon)$ that holds for every weighted grid. It cannot: no such bound exists for corridor-restricted search on risk-weighted grids.

\begin{theorem}[No instance-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ bound]\label{thm:no_bound}
For every $\epsilon > 0$ and every initial half-width $r_0 \geq 1$ there exists a risk-weighted $8$-connected grid (the model of Section~\ref{sec:method}) and a start--goal pair on which the incremental corridor procedure (Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils}) terminates at half-width $r^{\dagger} = r_0$ and returns a path whose cost ratio $C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}})/C(\pi^*)$ exceeds $1 + \epsilon$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\textit{Proof (explicit witness family, respecting $w = d + \lambda\rho \geq 1$).}
Fix $\epsilon > 0$, choose risk weight $\lambda > \epsilon$, and set $K := r_0 + 2$. Build a $(K{+}2)\times N$ grid (rows $0,\dots,K{+}1$; columns $0,\dots,N{-}1$) in the paper's $8$-connected model, with $w(u,v) = d(u,v) + \lambda\rho(v)$, $\rho \in [0,1]$: row~$1$ is free with \emph{maximal risk} $\rho \equiv 1$; row~$0$ is blocked; rows $2,\dots,K{-}1$ are blocked except at the two end columns $0$ and $N{-}1$, whose cells are free with risk~$0$ (the only vertical connectors); row~$K$ is the free \emph{safe lane} with risk~$0$; $s = (1,0)$, $g = (1,N{-}1)$. With row~$0$ blocked and rows $2,\dots,K{-}1$ blocked except at the ends, the diagonal moves available in the $8$-connected model create no in-corridor shortcut: from a row-$1$ cell every diagonal lands either in blocked row~$0$ or in a blocked interior cell of row~$2$, so in-corridor travel is confined to horizontal steps along row~$1$ (each costing $1+\lambda$). All edge costs lie in $[1, \sqrt2 + \lambda]$, a legal instance. With $r_0 = K{-}2$ the corridor $\mathcal{C}(r_0)$ covers rows $1,\dots,K{-}1$ but not row~$K$ (Chebyshev distance $K{-}1 = r_0{+}1$ from row~$1$); the in-corridor end-column connectors are dead-ends (they reach only the excluded row~$K$), so the only in-corridor $s$-to-$g$ path runs along row~$1$, of cost $(1+\lambda)(N{-}1) = \Theta(N)$. A path exists at $r_0$, so ILS returns it without widening: $C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}) = (1+\lambda)(N{-}1)$. The global optimum climbs the column-$0$ connector to the safe lane (cost $K{-}1$), traverses row~$K$ (cost $N{-}1$), and descends the column-$(N{-}1)$ connector to $g$ (cost $K{-}1$, the final step into the risk-$1$ cell $g$ costing $1+\lambda$): $C(\pi^*) \leq (N{-}1) + 2(K{-}1) + \lambda$. Hence
\[
\frac{C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}})}{C(\pi^*)} \;\geq\; \frac{(1+\lambda)(N{-}1)}{(N{-}1) + 2(K{-}1) + \lambda} \;\longrightarrow\; 1 + \lambda \quad (N \to \infty),
\]
which exceeds $1+\epsilon$ for $N$ large (the safe-lane detour is the global optimum once $\lambda(N{-}1) > 2(K{-}1) + \lambda$, i.e.\ for $N$ large). The construction is in the spirit of the no-uniform-suboptimality argument of \citet{Pohl1970}, transplanted to the geometric corridor-restriction setting. The two paths compared here use only cardinal moves with per-step cost in $[1, 1+\lambda]$, so for \emph{fixed} $\lambda$ the cost ratio is bounded by $1+\lambda$; unboundedness over the family therefore comes from $\lambda \to \infty$, not from $N$, which is the precise sense in which no instance-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ bound holds. A direct cell-count check ($K=3$, $\lambda=9$, $N=1000$) gives $\frac{10 \cdot 999}{999 + 4 + 9} = \frac{9990}{1012} = 9.87$, consistent with the asymptotic $1+\lambda = 10$. $\square$

The theorem resolves, in the negative, whether a distribution-agnostic tight bound is attainable; it also explains why the empirical safety net is the incremental expansion mechanism rather than an a-priori ratio guarantee. The a-posteriori certificate of Proposition~\ref{prop:certificate} is the constructive counterpart: although no uniform bound exists, each individual query carries a computable bound on its own sub-optimality.
Deriving tight $(1+\epsilon)$ bounds \emph{conditioned} on specific smooth risk distributions (e.g.\ bounded-gradient fields) remains open.

\subsection{Complexity of Corridor-Restricted Search}\label{subsec:complexity}

We now quantify the search-space saving and the running time of the corridor methods. Let $D_\infty = \|s - g\|_\infty$ and let $r \geq 0$ be an integer half-width.

\begin{theorem}[Corridor-size bound]\label{thm:size}
Counting all grid cells, $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (4r+1)(D_\infty + 2r + 1)$. If the reference line is axis-aligned the sharper bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (2r+1)(D_\infty + 2r + 1)$ holds.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
Assume w.l.o.g.\ the dominant axis is the column axis, so the Bresenham line has one cell per column $c \in \{c_{\min},\dots,c_{\max}\}$ with $c_{\max} - c_{\min} = D_\infty$ and row value $y(c)$ monotone and $1$-Lipschitz. Every corridor cell lies in one of at most $D_\infty + 2r + 1$ columns. Fix a column $j$: a cell $(j,y) \in \mathcal{C}(r)$ requires a line cell $(c,y(c))$ with $c \in [j-r,j+r] \cap [c_{\min},c_{\max}] =: [a,b]$ and $y \in [y(c)-r, y(c)+r]$. By monotonicity and $1$-Lipschitzness, $\{y(c): c \in [a,b]\} \subseteq [y(a), y(b)]$ with $y(b) - y(a) \leq b - a \leq 2r$, so the covered rows lie in $[y(a)-r, y(b)+r]$, at most $4r+1$ integers. Multiplying by the column count gives the bound; for an axis-aligned line $y(c)$ is constant and the per-column count is $2r+1$. $\square$

\begin{corollary}[Node-expansion reduction]\label{cor:reduction}
Dijkstra (or A* with an admissible, consistent heuristic) on $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}$ expands at most $|\mathcal{C}(r)|$ vertices in $O(|\mathcal{C}(r)| \log |\mathcal{C}(r)|)$ time. If the corridor contains a globally optimal path (no widening), this is $O(r D_\infty + r^2)$ expansions. On a square $N \times N$ grid with $D_\infty = \Theta(N)$ and $r = \alpha N$ one has $|\mathcal{C}(r)| = \Theta(\alpha N^2)$ (the bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:size} is matched from below: the band contains at least $(2r+1)$ cells in each of $\Theta(N)$ columns), so restricted search expands $\Theta(\alpha N^2)$ vertices, a $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ reduction relative to the $\Theta(N^2)$ worst case of unrestricted search.
\end{corollary}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
A consistent-heuristic A* (or Dijkstra) settles each vertex once, so expansions $\leq |\mathcal{C}(r)|$; the heap performs $O(|\mathcal{C}(r)|)$ operations each $O(\log|\mathcal{C}(r)|)$. Theorem~\ref{thm:size} gives $|\mathcal{C}(r)| = O(\alpha N^2)$, and the matching lower bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \geq (2r+1)\,D_\infty = \Omega(\alpha N^2)$ holds because the $\geq 2r+1$ cells stacked on each of the $D_\infty+1$ reference-line columns are distinct. Hence $|\mathcal{C}(r)| = \Theta(\alpha N^2)$, and the reduction relative to the $\Theta(N^2)$ worst case is $\Theta(1/\alpha)$; with $h_\lambda$, which is not consistent, the per-vertex single-expansion bound requires $h_0$ or Dijkstra. $\square$

\begin{theorem}[Complexity of ILS, AILS, RILS]\label{thm:complexity}
Let $N_{\!*} = \max(H,W)$ and let binary-heap Dijkstra, or A* with a consistent heuristic, cost $T_{\mathcal{A}}(n) = O(n \log n)$ on $n$ vertices of a bounded-degree grid. Then: \textup{(i)} the Bresenham line costs $O(N_{\!*})$ and the AILS/RILS summed-area table costs $O(HW)$ time and space with $O(1)$ windowed queries; \textup{(ii)} iteration $k$ costs $O(HW) + T_{\mathcal{A}}(|\mathcal{C}(r_k)|)$ (dense mask), or $O(|\mathcal{C}(r_k)|)$ with a corridor-local mask; \textup{(iii)} the loop runs at most $\min(K_{\max}, \lceil (r_{\mathrm{cover}}-r_0)/\Delta r \rceil + 1) = O(\min(K_{\max}, N_{\!*}/\Delta r))$ iterations, where $r_{\mathrm{cover}} = N_{\!*} - 1$ guarantees $\mathcal{C}(r_{\mathrm{cover}}) \supseteq V$; \textup{(iv)} worst case the time is $O(N_{\!*} \cdot HW \log(HW))$, i.e.\ $O(N^3 \log N)$ on a square grid, with $O(HW) = O(N^2)$ space; the favourable single-corridor case is $O(HW)$ preprocessing plus $O(|\mathcal{C}(r_0)| \log |\mathcal{C}(r_0)|)$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
(i) Bresenham emits one cell per dominant-axis step; the summed-area table is a two-pass prefix sum over $HW$ entries, each rectangular query four look-ups. (ii) A dense mask initialises $HW$ entries; the base search runs on $|\mathcal{C}(r_k)| \leq HW$ vertices. (iii) The half-width increases by $\Delta r \geq 1$ per failed attempt and caps at $K_{\max}$; every cell is within Chebyshev distance $N_{\!*}-1$ of an endpoint of $\ell(s,g)$, hence of $\ell(s,g)$, so $\mathcal{C}(N_{\!*}-1) \supseteq V$. (iv) Sum (ii) over $O(N_{\!*})$ iterations with $|\mathcal{C}(r_k)| \leq HW$; one mask and one table are reused, giving $O(HW)$ space. $\square$

\subsection{Geometric Containment Conditions}\label{subsec:containment}

For an $s$-to-$g$ path $\pi$ let $\zeta(\pi) = \max_{v \in \pi} d_\infty(v, \ell(s,g))$, and let $\zeta^\star = \min\{\zeta(\pi^*): \pi^* \text{ globally optimal}\}$.

\begin{lemma}[Containment]\label{lem:containment}
If $r \geq \zeta^\star$ then some globally optimal path lies in $\mathcal{C}(r)$, and optimal base search on $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}$ returns a globally optimal path.
\end{lemma}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
Let $\pi^*$ attain $\zeta(\pi^*) = \zeta^\star \leq r$; then $\pi^* \subseteq \mathcal{C}(r)$ and its edges lie in $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}$. The base search is optimal on the finite graph $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}$, so it returns a path of cost $\leq C(\pi^*)$, hence equal to the global minimum. $\square$

\begin{corollary}[Free-line optimality]\label{cor:freeline}
If $\lambda = 0$ and the Bresenham line $\ell(s,g)$ is obstacle-free, then $\zeta^\star = 0$ and ILS returns a global optimum at $r = 0$.
\end{corollary}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
For $\lambda = 0$, $C(\pi) \geq h(s,g)$ for every path. The Bresenham line uses $\min(|\Delta x|,|\Delta y|)$ diagonal and $\bigl||\Delta x|-|\Delta y|\bigr|$ cardinal steps, of total cost $\sqrt2 \min(|\Delta x|,|\Delta y|) + \bigl||\Delta x|-|\Delta y|\bigr| = h(s,g)$; being obstacle-free it is a geodesic in $V$, so $\zeta^\star = 0$. $\square$

\begin{proposition}[Isolated $d_\infty$-ball obstacle: feasibility]\label{prop:isolated_feas}
Let $\lambda = 0$ and let the obstacle set lie in a single Chebyshev ball $B = \{v: d_\infty(v,p_0) \leq b\}$ centred at a reference cell $p_0 \in \ell(s,g)$, with $s,g \notin B$ and the free region $\{v: d_\infty(v,\ell) \leq b+1\}\setminus B$ $8$-connected and containing $s,g$. Then a feasible $s$-to-$g$ path of deviation $\leq b+1$ exists, so ILS terminates with a solution at $r = b+1$ that is cost-optimal within $\mathcal{C}(b+1)$.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
The path that follows $\ell$ until meeting $B$, routes along the outer ring $\{v: d_\infty(v,p_0) = b+1\}$, then rejoins $\ell$, has deviation $\leq b+1$ (ring cells satisfy $d_\infty(v,\ell) \leq d_\infty(v,p_0) = b+1$) and is feasible, so $G|_{\mathcal{C}(b+1)}$ admits an $s$-to-$g$ path and the base search returns the in-corridor optimum. $\square$

\begin{conjecture}[Isolated convex obstacle: global optimality]\label{cj:convex}
Under the hypotheses of Proposition~\ref{prop:isolated_feas}, $\zeta^\star \leq b+1$, so ILS returns a \emph{global} optimum at $r = b+1$. A full proof needs an octile-metric exchange argument re-routing any optimal detour onto the ring without increasing cost; we have verified this numerically on all tested isolated-ball instances but do not yet have a general proof.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{conjecture}[Bounded-gradient deviation]\label{cj:gradient}
On an obstacle-free grid with $\Gamma$-Lipschitz risk ($|\rho(u)-\rho(v)| \leq \Gamma d_\infty(u,v)$), the least-deviating optimal path satisfies $\zeta^\star \leq f(\lambda,\Gamma)$ for some $f$ independent of grid size; by Lemma~\ref{lem:containment} the corridor cost slack is then exactly $0$ for all $r \geq f(\lambda,\Gamma)$. The constant $f$ is open; the uniform case $\Gamma = 0$ gives $f = 0$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:freeline}).
\end{conjecture}

\subsection{Soundness Boundary: Symmetry Pruning on Weighted Grids}\label{subsec:jps_boundary}

The corridor mechanism restricts the \emph{vertex set} and therefore preserves in-corridor optimality for any cost function (Lemma~\ref{lem:containment}). Symmetry-pruning methods such as JPS instead prune \emph{moves} under a uniform-cost assumption, and that assumption is essential.

\begin{lemma}[JPS is unsound under vertex weights]\label{lem:jps_unsound}
There is a vertex-weighted $8$-connected grid on which Jump Point Search, using the canonical pruning rules of \citet{Harabor2011}, returns a path of cost strictly greater than the optimum.
\end{lemma}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
Take the obstacle-free $3\times4$ grid, $s = (2,0)$, $g = (0,3)$, $\lambda = 1$, $\rho(0,2) = 1$ and $\rho \equiv 0$ elsewhere. The octile distance $h(s,g) = 2\sqrt2 + 1$ is realised by exactly three monotone geodesics; two of them, $P_2 = (2,0)(1,1)(1,2)(0,3)$ and $P_3 = (2,0)(2,1)(1,2)(0,3)$, avoid $(0,2)$ and cost $2\sqrt2+1$, while $P_1 = (2,0)(1,1)(0,2)(0,3)$ costs $2\sqrt2 + 2$. Since the grid is obstacle-free, no cell has a forced neighbour, so a straight scan halts only at the goal or a boundary and a diagonal scan generates a successor only where a straight sub-scan finds a jump point or the goal is intercepted. We trace every jump-point successor reachable from $s=(2,0)$ toward $g$. The cardinal scans from $s$ dead-end: rightward along row~$2$ reaches the boundary with no forced neighbour (so $(2,1)$ is scanned but \emph{not} generated as a successor and is never itself expanded), and upward along column~$0$ likewise. The diagonal (up-right) scan from $s$ steps to $(1,1)$, where its straight sub-scans (rightward through $(1,2),(1,3)$; upward through $(0,1)$) find no forced neighbour and no goal interception, so it continues to $(0,2)$ on the top boundary; from $(0,2)$ the rightward scan intercepts the goal column and yields $g=(0,3)$. Thus the only successors ever inserted are $(1,1)$, $(0,2)$, and $g$, none of which is $(1,2)$; and because $(2,1)$ is never expanded, no diagonal from $(2,1)$ to $(1,2)$ is ever launched. Hence $(1,2)$ is never generated as a jump-point successor. Both optimal paths $P_2,P_3$ pass through $(1,2)$, so neither lies in the jump-point graph, and every $s$-to-$g$ route in that graph passes through $(0,2)$ at cost $\geq 2\sqrt2 + 2 > 2\sqrt2 + 1$. JPS therefore returns a strictly suboptimal path. $\square$

\begin{remark}
JPS optimality on uniform-cost grids rests on a symmetry lemma: every optimal path has a same-\emph{length} canonical representative through jump points. Under vertex weights equal length no longer implies equal cost, so the canonical representative ($P_1$) can be costlier than the optimum ($P_2$, $P_3$). This is why the present work restricts JPS to $\lambda = 0$ and adopts vertex-set (corridor) restriction as the weighted-grid mechanism. The result also bounds what a JPS-inside-corridor hybrid can claim: such a hybrid is sound only on the uniform-cost portions of the cost landscape.
\end{remark}

\section{Experimental Design}\label{sec:experiments}
%% ====================================================================

We carried out sixteen experiments (numbered 1--14, with 10b, 10c as benchmark variants) to evaluate corridor-constrained pathfinding on risk-weighted grids, supplemented by two D*~Lite comparison runs labelled 4b and 5b that are reported alongside Experiments~4 and~5 in the Discussion.
Experiments 1 to 5 form the core evaluation. Experiments 6 to 9 cover additional baselines, sensitivity analysis, and the RILS algorithm. Experiments 10, 10b, and 10c validate the results on standard Moving AI Lab benchmarks, including risk-annotated and maze topologies. Experiment 11 evaluates heuristic sensitivity. Experiment 12 validates the methods on a real container terminal (Penang Port, Malaysia) rasterised from public OpenStreetMap data, addressing external-validity concerns about the procedurally generated grids used in Experiments~1--11. Experiment 13 tests autopilot-mission feasibility by converting ILS paths into ArduPilot-compatible waypoint missions and evaluating their compactness and smoothness. Experiment 14 validates the complexity theory (corridor-size bound and $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ reduction) on grids up to $2000 \times 2000$ and establishes C++/Python implementation parity. Supplementary Experiments 4b and 5b directly compare D*~Lite against ILS on clustered re-planning and progressive missions respectively.
The experiments all use 8-connected grid movement with the octile distance heuristic, adjusted per Eq.~\eqref{eq:heuristic} when $\lambda > 0$.
Because node-count and runtime distributions are right-skewed, significance is assessed with the non-parametric Wilcoxon signed-rank test on paired samples, with Holm correction for family-wise error across the configurations of each experiment; effect sizes are reported as the matched-pairs rank-biserial correlation, and confidence intervals are $95\%$ percentile bootstrap CIs ($10{,}000$ resamples). The significance threshold is the Holm-corrected $\alpha = 0.05$. All node-count differences reported as significant below survive this correction.
Experiments~1--12 were run on an Intel Core i7 system with 16~GB RAM; the waypoint-mission feasibility sweep (Experiment~13) was run on an Apple MacBook Pro (M1~Pro). Wall-clock comparisons are therefore made only within an experiment, never across hardware; the implementation-independent node-count metric is comparable throughout.

\paragraph{Baseline scope.}
The baselines are chosen one per acceleration \emph{category}: heuristic inflation (Weighted A*), incremental repair (D*~Lite), and symmetry pruning (JPS), against the corridor's spatial-restriction category. Three further methods are deliberately out of scope. Goal bounding-box pruning~\citep{Rabin2016} and geometric containers~\citep{Wagner2005} are the closest spatial-restriction competitors but require all-edges preprocessing of a \emph{static} graph, so they do not apply to the dynamic re-planning experiments (4, 4b, 5, 5b) that motivate the corridor's preprocessing-free design; on the static experiments they are a fair comparison and we flag this as the most important open baseline. Theta*~\citep{Nash2007} and Field~D*~\citep{Ferguson2005} solve the any-angle and continuous-cost problems respectively, a different problem class from the 8-connected discrete-cost setting here. We note these omissions explicitly rather than implying the comparison set is exhaustive.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Risk-Annotated Grids}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We generated $200 \times 200$ binary occupancy grids at three obstacle densities (10\%, 20\%, 30\%), each overlaid with one of three risk distributions: (i)~\emph{gradient} (risk increasing linearly from top-left to bottom-right), (ii)~\emph{hotspot} (Gaussian-centred high-risk zones), and (iii)~\emph{uniform} (constant risk across all cells).
Six risk-weight values were tested:
\[
\lambda \in \{0,\; 0.1,\; 0.5,\; 1.0,\; 2.0,\; 5.0\}.
\]
For each density--risk--$\lambda$ configuration, 100 random maps were generated, for a total of 5,400 planned paths.
Start was fixed at $(0,0)$ and goal at $(199, 199)$.

\paragraph{Algorithms.}
Standard A* (baseline) and ILS~A* (5\% initial corridor width, up to 10 expansion attempts) were compared.
Metrics: runtime (ms), nodes expanded, path cost ratio (ILS/A*), cumulative risk exposure ratio, and corridor expansion count.

\paragraph{Statistical protocol.}
Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Holm correction and matched-pairs rank-biserial effect sizes were computed for node-count differences at each configuration (Cohen's $d$ is additionally reported for continuity with the prior ILS study).
Significance threshold: Holm-corrected $p < 0.05$.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Port Environment Validation}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Procedurally generated container-port grids reproduced the structural features of real port facilities: a quay zone (sparse, open), a container yard (rectangular blocks separated by narrow aisles), a gate zone (barriers with controlled openings), and a buffer zone (mixed clutter).
Biosecurity risk layers were overlaid, with elevated risk near container blocks (inspection zones) and gate checkpoints.
Three grid sizes ($200 \times 200$, $300 \times 300$, $500 \times 500$) and three risk weights ($\lambda \in \{0, 0.5, 1.0\}$) were tested.
Thirty start--goal pairs were evaluated per configuration (270 total).

\paragraph{Port generation parameters.}
The procedural port generator uses the following zone proportions relative to grid height: quay zone $0$--$15\%$, buffer $15$--$20\%$, container yard $20$--$70\%$, gate zone $75$--$85\%$, open area $85$--$100\%$.
Container blocks in the yard zone are $8$--$15$ cells wide and $3$--$6$ cells tall, separated by aisles of $2$--$3$ cells.
Gate barriers span the full grid width with $3$--$5$ controlled openings per barrier, each $3$--$5$ cells wide.
Risk values are assigned as: $0.7$--$0.9$ adjacent to container blocks (inspection zones), $0.5$--$0.7$ at gate checkpoints, $0.1$--$0.3$ in open/quay areas.

\paragraph{Algorithms.}
Standard A*, ILS~A*, and AILS~A* ($r_{\min} = 2$, $r_{\max} = \lceil 0.1 \cdot \min(H, W) \rceil$, $\gamma = 1.0$, $\omega = 3$).

\subsection{Experiment 3: JPS Comparative Evaluation}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To quantify how corridor-based methods compare with symmetry-breaking techniques on uniform-cost grids, we evaluated A*, ILS~A* (3\% corridor), and JPS on $200 \times 200$ grids at three densities (10\%, 20\%, 30\%) with 100 maps per density.
No risk layer was applied ($\lambda = 0$).
All algorithms, including JPS, were implemented from scratch in Python following the canonical descriptions in \citet{Harabor2011} for JPS and \citet{Hart1968} for A*.
Custom implementations were used rather than established libraries to ensure a fair comparison under identical language overhead and data structures.

\subsection{Experiment 4: Dynamic Re-planning}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To simulate re-planning after obstacle discovery, we computed an initial path on each grid, then inserted 5 obstacle clusters (radius 3 cells) along the path at approximately 40\% of its length.
Re-planning was initiated from the agent's pre-insertion position.
We compared A* full re-run and AILS corridor-constrained re-plan on $200 \times 200$ grids at three densities with 128 maps per density.

\subsection{Experiment 5: Progressive Obstacle Discovery}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Each simulated mission placed an agent at $(0,0)$ on a $200 \times 200$ grid with goal $(199, 199)$.
Every 20 steps, the agent's sensor (range 15 cells) discovered 3 new contamination points, each blocking a $3 \times 3$ cell cluster.
If the path ahead was blocked, the agent re-planned from its current position.
Sixty missions per density level (10\%, 20\%, 30\%) were simulated, producing approximately 12 re-plan events per mission.
Both A* and ILS~A* were evaluated.

\subsection{Experiment 6: Weighted A* Comparison}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To compare corridor-based restriction with heuristic-inflation-based acceleration, we evaluated Weighted A* (wA*) with weights $w \in \{1.5,\, 2.0,\, 3.0\}$ against A*, ILS, and AILS on $200 \times 200$ risk-annotated grids.
The same density--risk--$\lambda$ configurations as Experiment~1 were used, with 50 random maps per configuration.
Start and goal were placed at random positions (minimum Manhattan distance 100 cells) to diversify the path topology.

\subsection{Experiment 7: Corridor Width Sensitivity}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To characterise the sensitivity of ILS performance to corridor width, we swept the initial corridor fraction $\alpha$ across nine values,
\[
\alpha \in \{0.01,\; 0.02,\; 0.03,\; 0.05,\; 0.07,\; 0.10,\; 0.15,\; 0.20,\; 0.30\},
\]
on $200 \times 200$ risk-annotated grids at three densities (10\%, 20\%, 30\%) and two risk distributions (gradient and hotspot) at two risk weights ($\lambda \in \{0.5, 1.0\}$).
Thirty maps per configuration were evaluated, for a total of 3,240 planned paths.
Uniform risk was omitted from this sweep because its spatially invariant cost landscape produced no measurable corridor sensitivity in pilot runs; the gradient and hotspot distributions bracket the practically relevant range.
Table~\ref{tab:exp7_sensitivity} reports grand means over all density--risk--$\lambda$ configurations.

\subsection{Experiment 8: RILS Evaluation}

\paragraph{Setup.}
RILS (Section~\ref{subsec:rils}) was compared against A*, ILS, and AILS on $200 \times 200$ risk-annotated grids at three densities, three risk distributions, and three risk weights ($\lambda \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$).
Fifty maps per configuration with random start--goal pairs (minimum distance 100 cells).
RILS parameters: $r_{\text{base}} = 0.05 \cdot \text{diag}$, $r_{\max} = 0.15 \cdot \text{diag}$, $\beta = 1.0$, $\omega = 5$.

\subsection{Experiment 9: Large-Grid AILS Validation}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To resolve the AILS grid-size threshold effect observed in Experiment~2, we evaluated AILS on $500 \times 500$ risk-annotated grids at three densities, three risk distributions (gradient, hotspot, uniform), and two risk weights ($\lambda \in \{0.5, 1.0\}$).
Fifteen maps per configuration (270 planned paths in total).

\subsection{Experiment 10: Moving AI Lab Benchmark Evaluation}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To establish external validity and enable comparison with published results, we evaluated A*, ILS, and AILS on standard benchmark maps from the Moving AI Lab~\citep{Sturtevant2012}.
Three map categories were tested: (i)~\emph{game maps} from Dragon Age: Origins (4 maps: lak303d, den501d, brc202d, ost003d), featuring structured indoor environments with rooms and corridors; (ii)~\emph{mazes} (4 maps: maze512-$k$-0, $k \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$) with progressively wider corridors; and (iii)~\emph{random maps} (4 maps: random512-$d$-0, $d \in \{10, 20, 30, 40\}$\% density) matching the procedural grids used in Experiments~1--9.
All maps are $512 \times 512$ or smaller.
Up to 100 scenarios per map were selected from the official scenario files, preferring longer paths for a more meaningful evaluation.
No risk layer was applied ($\lambda = 0$); this experiment tests the corridor mechanism itself on diverse standard-benchmark topologies.
ILS used the default $\alpha = 0.05$; AILS used $r_{\min} = 0.02 \cdot \text{diag}$, $r_{\max} = 0.10 \cdot \text{diag}$.

\subsection{Experiment 10b: Risk-Annotated Benchmark Evaluation}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To address the limitation of testing benchmarks only at $\lambda = 0$, we overlaid synthetic risk layers (gradient and hotspot distributions) on Moving AI Lab random maps and evaluated ILS, AILS, and RILS at $\lambda \in \{0.5, 1.0\}$.
Two random maps (random512-20-0, random512-30-0) were tested with 10 scenarios per configuration, selecting longer paths for meaningful evaluation.

\subsection{Experiment 10c: Maze Benchmark Evaluation}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To stress-test corridor methods on adversarial topologies, we evaluated ILS and AILS on procedurally generated maze maps of size $256 \times 256$ with four corridor widths ($k \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$), corresponding to obstacle densities of 51\%, 34\%, 21\%, and 14\%.
Twenty scenarios per maze configuration were tested with no risk layer ($\lambda = 0$).
Mazes were generated using randomised DFS, producing perfect mazes where every cell is reachable.
Official Moving AI Lab maze maps could not be used due to persistent server-side download errors (HTTP 500) at the time of experiments; multiple download attempts across different dates returned error pages rather than map data.

\subsection{Experiment 11: Multi-Heuristic Comparison}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To evaluate robustness to heuristic choice, we compared four heuristics within ILS at $\lambda = 1.0$: (i)~octile distance, (ii)~Euclidean distance, (iii)~Manhattan distance, and (iv)~risk-adjusted octile ($h_\lambda = h \cdot (1 + \lambda \cdot 0.3)$).
Each heuristic was tested with both standard A* and ILS ($\alpha = 0.05$) on $200 \times 200$ risk-annotated grids at three densities and three risk distributions (20 maps per configuration).

\subsection{Experiment 12: Real Port Validation (Penang Port)}\label{subsec:exp12_setup}

\paragraph{Setup.}
To address the external-validity concern that all prior experiments used procedurally generated grids, we evaluated all five algorithms (A*, JPS, ILS, AILS, RILS) on real port facilities derived from public geospatial data.
The study site is the North Butterworth Container Terminal (NBCT), Penang Port, Malaysia, operated by Penang Port Sdn Bhd.
A $500 \times 500$ occupancy grid was rasterised from OpenStreetMap (OSM) vector data within the geographic bounding box $5.3950^\circ$--$5.4150^\circ$N, $100.3500^\circ$--$100.3800^\circ$E (approximately $2.2 \times 3.3$\,km).
OSM building footprints, breakwaters, and water bodies were marked as obstacles; OSM road geometry was preserved as free space.
A biosecurity risk layer was constructed by zone semantics: $0.7$--$0.9$ inside industrial/container polygons (inspection hotspots), $0.5$--$0.7$ at gate zones (controlled checkpoints, computed as the intersection of dilated obstacles with road geometry), and $0.05$--$0.20$ in open and quay areas.
A Gaussian falloff of standard deviation $25$ cells was applied around container zones to produce a realistic spatial gradient.

\paragraph{Procedure.}
Thirty random start--goal pairs were generated with a minimum separation of $40\%$ of the grid diagonal ($\sim 280$ cells) using a fixed random seed for reproducibility.
Each algorithm was evaluated at three risk weights: $\lambda \in \{0.0, 0.5, 1.0\}$.
JPS was reported only at $\lambda = 0$ (it does not operate on risk-weighted grids).
All algorithms used the same start--goal pairs at each $\lambda$ to enable paired comparison.
Metrics: wall-clock runtime (ms), nodes expanded, path cost ratio versus A*, and cumulative risk exposure ratio versus A*.

\subsection{Experiment 13: Autopilot Waypoint-Mission Feasibility}\label{subsec:exp13_setup}

\paragraph{Setup.}
All prior experiments measure planning in isolation. To test whether corridor-restricted paths are convertible into a deployable autopilot mission, we implemented the ArduPilot-compatible mission pipeline: planned grid paths are converted to GPS waypoints (1\,m per cell), compressed by collinear-waypoint removal, and assembled into a waypoint mission with a 2\,m waypoint-proximity threshold and a return-to-launch terminator. We evaluate the pipeline in a deterministic mission simulator that reproduces the waypoint-following geometry; we do \emph{not} claim a closed-loop flight in the ArduCopter SITL physics simulator, which is identified as the immediate next validation step (Section~\ref{subsec:practical}). A representative mission used a $50 \times 50$ grid at $20\%$ obstacle density.

\paragraph{Parameter sweep.}
Beyond the single mission, we ran a systematic feasibility sweep (100 independent maps per configuration) over three deployment parameters: grid size ($30$ to $100$ cells), obstacle density ($10\%$ to $30\%$), and initial corridor width ($3\%$ to $15\%$). Metrics: planning time, node reduction, path optimality ratio, waypoint count after compression, and mean heading change between consecutive waypoints (path smoothness). The sweep characterises the small-grid flight-envelope regime, which is below the AILS wall-clock crossover ($\sim 300 \times 300$) established in Experiment~9.

\subsection{Experiment 14: Complexity-Bound Validation and Implementation Parity}\label{subsec:exp14_setup}

\paragraph{Setup.}
This experiment validates the theoretical results of Sections~\ref{subsec:complexity}--\ref{subsec:jps_boundary} directly and settles the implementation confound. It has two parts. \emph{(a) Bound and scaling validation:} on procedurally generated risk grids of size $N \times N$ for $N \in \{200, 300, 500, 750, 1000, 1500, 2000\}$ we (i) verify the corridor-size bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (4r+1)(D_\infty+2r+1)$ on every (reference-line, $r$) case, and (ii) measure the node-expansion reduction $\text{nodes(A*)}/\text{nodes(ILS)}$ and the space ratio $|V|/|\mathcal{C}|$ as functions of the corridor fraction $\alpha$ and grid size, testing the $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ prediction of Corollary~\ref{cor:reduction}. \emph{(b) Implementation parity:} we re-implemented A*, ILS, AILS, and a canonical JPS in C++17 sharing the same instance loader, and verified on 3{,}000 paired A*-family instances ($256 \times 256$ grids, mixed density and risk) that the C++ and Python references return identical path cost and identical node-expansion counts (up to equal-$f$ tie ordering). The C++ reference and the parity harness are released with the code.

Fig.~\ref{fig:speedup_lambda} reveals a clear pattern: speedup grows with $\lambda$ for gradient risk, plateauing beyond $\lambda = 2.0$.

Gradient distributions at 30\% density achieved comparable speedups to 10\% density ($7.83\times$ vs.\ $7.90\times$), which suggests that corridor efficiency on gradient risk is robust across obstacle densities.

\subsubsection{Node Reduction}

Node reduction followed the same $\lambda$-dependent pattern.
At $\lambda = 0$, reductions ranged from $0$--$31\%$ (pure corridor effect, with higher-density grids benefiting more from corridor restriction even without risk weighting).
At $\lambda = 1.0$, reductions reached $41$--$84\%$, with gradient risk consistently yielding the highest reductions ($83$--$84\%$ across all densities) and hotspot risk the lowest ($41$--$51\%$).
At $\lambda \geq 2.0$, gradient risk reached a peak reduction of $86.1\%$ (uniform risk peaked at $84.7\%$), meaning ILS explored fewer than 1 in 7 nodes compared to standard A*.
All node-count differences at $\lambda \geq 0.1$ were significant under the Holm-corrected Wilcoxon test (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}). We do not emphasise parametric effect sizes here: node counts are near-deterministic across repeats of a fixed instance (often zero variance), so a standardised mean difference is uninformative (it can be arbitrarily large); the operationally meaningful quantity is the node-reduction \emph{ratio} reported throughout, with bootstrap CIs.

\subsubsection{Path Quality and Risk Exposure}

Path quality depended strongly on both risk distribution and $\lambda$:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Gradient risk:} Path cost ratio at most $1.001$ across all $\lambda$ values (suboptimality below $0.1\%$). The smooth risk landscape meant the corridor consistently captured near-optimal paths.
\item \textbf{Uniform risk:} Ratio at most $1.001$ for all configurations, that is, effectively optimal, consistent with the spatially invariant nature of uniform risk.
\item \textbf{Hotspot risk:} Ratio at most $1.013$ for $\lambda \leq 1.0$, degrading to $1.11$ at $\lambda = 5.0$ for 30\% density. The localised nature of hotspots created detours that occasionally fell outside the corridor.
\end{itemize}

Risk exposure (cumulative risk along the path) was nearly identical to A* for gradient and uniform distributions ($\leq 0.1\%$ difference).
For hotspot risk at high $\lambda$ ($\geq 2.0$), ILS exposure exceeded A* by up to $9.1\times$ at $\lambda = 5.0$ with 30\% density, because the tight corridor could not route around distant hotspots.
This exposure--speed tradeoff is inherent to corridor methods, and a mission planner can tune it: at moderate risk weights ($\lambda \leq 1.0$) the exposure penalty stays modest (below $2.5\times$), while at extreme weights the corridor buys speed by accepting markedly higher exposure.

\paragraph{Robustness to endpoint geometry.}
The fixed corner-to-corner endpoints used above favour a diagonal corridor, so we re-ran the same protocol with \emph{random} start--goal pairs (minimum separation $40\%$ of the diagonal; $100$ random maps per cell across the $18$ density--risk--$\lambda$ cells, i.e.\ $1{,}800$ nominal instances with $64$--$96$ solved per cell). At $\lambda = 1.0$ (nine cells), three things change and one does not. The corridor advantage persists (ILS $3.17\times$ mean speedup, $58.8\%$ node reduction; AILS $4.00\times$, $65.8\%$), with mean cost ratios of $1.004$ (ILS) and $1.007$ (AILS); near-optimality holds \emph{on average} rather than uniformly, as the densest hotspot cells reach $1.011$ (ILS) and $1.019$ (AILS). So the advantage is not an artefact of the corner geometry. But the \emph{ranking flips}: with random endpoints AILS overtakes ILS ($4.00\times$ against $3.17\times$), because the diverse path topologies let its density-adaptive width pay off, whereas with fixed corners ILS leads (Experiments~1--5). And the \emph{magnitude} is lower: the $7.90\times$ peak is specific to the corner geometry; representative random-endpoint speedups are $3$ to $4\times$. We therefore report the corner-to-corner figures as a favourable best case and the random-endpoint figures as the representative operating point, and we recommend AILS over ILS whenever path requests are diverse.

\paragraph{D*~Lite comparison.}
A supplementary experiment (Experiment~4b) compared D*~Lite against A* re-run and ILS on the same clustered-insertion re-planning task across 83--69 valid maps per density.
\emph{Important implementation note:} all algorithms (A*, ILS, D*~Lite) were implemented in Python using identical data structures. The distinction matters: D*~Lite's theoretical edge (fewer node expansions) does not become a wall-clock win in an interpreted implementation when changes are clustered, because the priority-queue bookkeeping costs more than it saves, whereas the corridor restriction is cheap enough that its node savings survive.
In compiled implementations, D*~Lite's node reduction would likely yield runtime benefits, particularly for small, frequent perturbations.

The likely reason is that AILS's density-adaptive corridor handles diverse path topologies better, because the straight-line reference between random endpoints often crosses regions of varying obstacle density.

Table~\ref{tab:exp10_benchmark} shows that corridor-based methods provide no speedup on standard benchmark maps at $\lambda = 0$ (that is, with no risk layer), and they are substantially slower on game maps.

\paragraph{Game maps (Dragon Age: Origins).}
On game maps with complex indoor topologies (60 to 83\% obstacle density), ILS was $2.7$ to $3.4$ times slower than A* ($0.29\times$ to $0.37\times$ speedup), with strongly negative node reduction (between $-170\%$ and $-234\%$).
AILS performed slightly better ($0.32\times$ to $0.46\times$) but was still substantially slower.
The cause is straightforward. Game maps consist of rooms connected by narrow doorways, and the Bresenham reference line between start and goal crosses many walls.
Each failed corridor attempt expands the search, and the accumulated effort across multiple attempts far exceeds A*'s single search.
Solve rates were also lower (49 to 94\%) because some scenarios require circuitous paths that never come close to the Bresenham line.

\paragraph{Random maps (10--40\% density).}
On random maps at $\lambda = 0$, ILS performed near-neutrally ($0.94$--$1.10\times$), with positive node reduction only at 40\% density ($12.5\%$).
This is consistent with Experiment~1 at $\lambda = 0$, where ILS speedup was $0.90$--$1.37\times$: corridor-based methods provide minimal benefit on uniform-cost grids because the cost landscape is simple and the optimal path lies close to the geometric shortest path, leaving little for the corridor to prune.
All scenarios were solved with optimal paths (cost ratio $\leq 1.0001$).

\paragraph{Implications.}
This is the cleanest evidence for a claim that runs through the paper: the corridor earns its keep from the risk-weighted cost landscape, not from the corridor mechanism on its own.
On standard uniform-cost benchmarks, the corridor adds overhead without proportional benefit.
The $3\times$ to $8\times$ speedups seen in Experiments 1 and 6 to 8 arise specifically because the risk-weighted cost function ($\lambda > 0$) creates search landscapes in which a spatial corridor along the reference line prunes the nodes that A* would otherwise explore to evaluate risk-avoiding detours.
Corridor methods are best read, then, as a targeted accelerator for risk-weighted pathfinding rather than a general-purpose speedup for any grid.

\paragraph{Full-corpus extension and a topological boundary.}
To test this at scale we evaluated the \emph{entire} Dragon Age: Origins corpus --- all $156$ maps, $7{,}800$ scenarios (longest paths per map), suboptimality scored against the published \texttt{.scen} optima under the no-corner-cutting convention. At $\lambda = 0$ the corridor methods are slower than A* as expected. Adding a gradient risk layer ($\lambda = 1.0$) raises their solve rate (ILS $56.0\%$, AILS $50.4\%$) and keeps path quality near-optimal (mean suboptimality $0.09\%$ for ILS, $0.16\%$ for AILS), but the mean node-expansion reduction \emph{remains negative} (ILS $-39.7\%$, AILS $-11.9\%$). This refines the activation claim of Experiment~10b: risk weighting activates the corridor advantage on \emph{open/random} topologies (where the optimal path tracks the start--goal line), but \emph{not} on the room-and-corridor topology of DAO, where the optimal path deviates far from the Bresenham line. The containment analysis of Section~\ref{subsec:containment} explains why: DAO instances have a large minimal deviation $\zeta^\star$, so the corridor has to widen a long way (or give up) before it contains an optimal path, and weighting the cost by risk does nothing to shrink that geometric deviation. The corridor advantage thus has \emph{two} preconditions, not one: a risk-weighted cost function \emph{and} a topology in which the optimal path stays near the reference line.

\emph{First, the risk-weighted activation pattern transfers to real data.}
At $\lambda = 0$ the corridor methods produce little or no benefit (ILS $0.96\times$, RILS $0.95\times$, both with $\approx 4\%$ node reduction), and even AILS only reaches $1.58\times$. As $\lambda$ increases to $0.5$ and $1.0$, ILS climbs to $1.61\times$ then $2.05\times$, AILS to $2.90\times$ then $3.44\times$. The corridor advantage is triggered by the risk-weighted cost landscape, not by the topology --- exactly the conclusion drawn from Experiment~10b on Moving AI Lab maps, here confirmed on a real port grid from OpenStreetMap.

\emph{Second, AILS dominates speedup on the real port.}
On the $500 \times 500$ Penang grid AILS achieved $3.44\times$ wall-clock speedup at $\lambda = 1.0$ with $70.3\%$ node reduction. This is consistent with the large-grid AILS results in Experiment~9 (mean $5.3\times$, $66.8\%$ reduction on $500 \times 500$ synthetic grids) and with the procedural-port results in Experiment~2 ($1.94\times$ AILS speedup at $\lambda = 1.0$ on $500 \times 500$). The OSM-derived topology, with its mix of dense container blocks and open quay/road regions, plays to AILS's density-adaptive corridor.

\emph{Third, RILS preserves near-optimal path quality at the cost of speedup.}
At $\lambda = 0.5$ and $1.0$ RILS produced cost ratios of $1.0000$ and $1.0002$ respectively, against $1.005$--$1.011$ for ILS and $1.010$--$1.018$ for AILS. The penalty for this quality is a speedup that stays near $1.0\times$. This matches Experiment~8's finding that RILS is the path-quality-oriented variant in the corridor family. On the real port, where high-risk zones cluster around container blocks and gate areas, the wider risk-responsive corridor of RILS captures almost exactly the same paths A* would take, while ILS and AILS occasionally clip a risk-avoidance detour.

A separate observation concerns JPS on the real port topology. JPS achieved $96.7\%$ node reduction at $\lambda = 0$ but only $0.55\times$ wall-clock speedup, with path cost identical to A* (cost ratio $1.000$, confirming JPS's optimality on uniform-cost grids). The wall-clock discrepancy follows the same Python-overhead pattern reported for D*~Lite in Experiment~4b: JPS's recursive jump-scan procedure incurs substantial constant-factor cost in the interpreted Python implementation, which dominates the savings from its aggressive symmetry pruning. A compiled-language port would be expected to convert the node-reduction win into a wall-clock win, consistent with the discussion in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}. JPS is not applicable at $\lambda > 0$, so the comparison at $\lambda \in \{0.5, 1.0\}$ falls to the corridor methods.

So Experiment~12 carries the corridor mechanism off the procedural grids and onto a real port rasterised from public OSM data: the AILS speed advantage and the RILS quality advantage both survive the move, and the risk-weighted activation condition turns out to be a property of the cost landscape, not an artefact of how we generate grids. The grid is publicly reproducible from the bounding box and rasterisation procedure released with the code.

\subsection{Experiment 13: Autopilot Waypoint-Mission Feasibility Results}\label{subsec:exp13_results}

The $50 \times 50$ ILS path converted cleanly into a deployable mission: a compact GPS waypoint sequence with a 2\,m proximity threshold and a return-to-launch terminator, followed by the mission simulator with smooth heading transitions and no post-processing beyond collinear-waypoint compression. A 100-map sweep over grid size ($30$--$100$), density ($10$--$30\%$) and corridor width ($3$--$15\%$) confirmed that the generated missions stay geometrically clean. Path optimality stayed within $0.2\%$ of the A* optimum up to $25\%$ density (mean cost ratio rising from $1.0001$ at $10\%$ to $1.0019$ at $25\%$) and degraded only mildly to $1.0035$ at $30\%$; collinear compression removed $\approx 69\%$ of the waypoints, and the mean heading change between consecutive waypoints was $\approx 13^\circ$. \subsection{Experiment 14: Complexity-Bound Validation and Parity Results}\label{subsec:exp14_results}

\emph{Corridor-size bound.} Across $336$ (reference-line, $r$) cases spanning $N \in \{200,\dots,2000\}$, the bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (4r+1)(D_\infty+2r+1)$ held with \emph{zero violations}; the tightest case reached $98.6\%$ of the bound, confirming both its validity and that it is not loose.

\emph{$\Theta(1/\alpha)$ node-expansion reduction.} Table~\ref{tab:exp14_scaling} and Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} report the node-reduction factor at four corridor fractions across grid sizes. The reduction is essentially \emph{invariant to $N$} from $200$ to $2000$ at each $\alpha$ (e.g.\ $6.0$--$6.4\times$ at $\alpha=0.05$ across all sizes), while it scales as $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ across $\alpha$ ($1.7\times \to 3.2\times \to 6.2\times \to 15\times$ as $\alpha$ halves from $0.20$ to $0.02$). This is the empirical confirmation that the corridor advantage is a property of the corridor fraction, not of grid size.

The data-justified knee for the ILS corridor fraction (maximum node reduction subject to mean cost ratio $\leq 1.005$) was $\alpha_0 \in [0.02, 0.05]$, consistent with the default $\alpha_0 = 0.05$ used throughout and with the Experiment~7 Pareto analysis; AILS window $\omega$ and RILS exponent $\beta$ showed the monotone effects assumed in Section~\ref{sec:method}.

\emph{Compiled wall-clock.} To test whether the corridor wall-clock advantage is a Python artefact, we re-ran the clustered re-planning task ($\lambda = 1.0$, $5$-cluster batch, $10$ maps per size, median of $5$ repeats) on the C++ reference (Table~\ref{tab:exp14_cpp}). The advantage is not an artefact: at compiled speed ILS and AILS are $5.6$ to $7.5\times$ faster than a full A* re-run, and the factor is stable from $200^2$ to $1000^2$. The D*~Lite comparison also survives compilation: D*~Lite expands $40$--$47\%$ fewer nodes than A* but is $5$ to $7\times$ \emph{slower} in wall-clock even in C++, because its priority-queue maintenance dominates on a single large batch of changes. (Our C++ D*~Lite uses a \texttt{std::set} open list rather than an indexed heap, so its time is a conservative upper bound; a heap would shave a small constant factor but not close a $5$--$7\times$ gap on batch re-planning.) This settles the implementation confound for the cross-family wall-clock claims on the re-planning task; a compiled JPS comparison on the static uniform-cost task is the one remaining measurement.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Positioning}

The comparisons place corridor methods between heuristic inflation and incremental repair. They beat JPS in wall-clock on uniform-cost grids at density $\geq 20\%$ and run on cost functions JPS cannot touch; they keep mission success at $67$--$70\%$ where D*~Lite collapses to $17$--$42\%$ under clustered change; and they trade a little speed (against wA*'s $23$--$56\times$) for an order-of-magnitude better path quality ($0.3\%$ vs.\ $2$--$5\%$). RILS is the quality specialist (best optimality, lowest speedup); AILS overtakes ILS once endpoints are random and grids exceed $\sim 500 \times 500$, where its adaptive width pays for its integral-image overhead. The corridor is an acceleration \emph{layer}: any optimal base search slots inside it, and on low-density uniform grids JPS itself could serve as that base for multiplicative gains.

\subsection{Practical recommendations}\label{subsec:practical}

For routine surveillance, use ILS with $\lambda \in [0.5, 1.0]$ and $\alpha = 0.05$ ($3$--$7\times$ speedup, $\leq 0.3\%$ suboptimal). On grids $\geq 500 \times 500$, switch to AILS ($5.3\times$ mean vs.\ $3.5\times$); below that, ILS's simpler corridor avoids the overhead. When risk exposure tolerance is near zero, use RILS ($\leq 0.1\%$ suboptimal). For clustered re-planning, prefer ILS/AILS over D*~Lite; for time-critical work that tolerates $2$--$5\%$ suboptimality, Weighted A* with $w = 1.5$ ($7$--$43\times$) is the better tool.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several threats to validity should be read alongside the results. \emph{(i) Implementation.} All timings are from pure-Python implementations, and the Python-overhead caveat cuts both ways: where competitors win on node count but lose on wall-clock (D*~Lite, JPS) we attribute the loss to constant-factor overhead, so the corridor methods' own wall-clock wins carry the same caveat. Experiment~14 addresses this: a released C++ reference reproduces the Python node counts on $3{,}000$ A*-family instances, and a compiled re-planning benchmark (Table~\ref{tab:exp14_cpp}) confirms that the ILS/AILS wall-clock advantage ($5.6$--$7.5\times$ over A*) and the D*~Lite wall-clock loss both persist at compiled speed, so these are not Python artefacts. The one cross-family comparison not yet re-run compiled is JPS on the static uniform-cost task, so the ``matches/beats JPS'' observation should still be read as pure-Python evidence pending that measurement. The JPS node counts are moreover not directly comparable to A*-family expansions (Section~\ref{sec:results}). \emph{(ii) Risk realism.} The risk fields are synthetic everywhere, including the ``real'' ports, whose risk layers are assigned by rule from OSM polygon semantics rather than measured; Experiment~12 therefore validates transfer of the mechanism to real \emph{topology}, not to real risk, and the biosecurity setting is a motivating application rather than a validated capability. \emph{(iii) Endpoint geometry.} Experiments~1--5 use fixed corner-to-corner endpoints, which favour a diagonal corridor and on which ILS leads; with random endpoints (Experiments~6--8) AILS overtakes ILS, so the best-case figures (e.g.\ the $7.90\times$) are tied to the corner geometry and the variant ranking is endpoint-dependent. \emph{(iv) Heuristic.} The default $h_\lambda$ is conditionally admissible and, as Section~\ref{subsec:formal} and Table~\ref{tab:exp14_rho} show, frequently inadmissible at $\bar{\rho}=0.3$, so optimality claims are near-optimality unless $h_0$ is used. \emph{(v) Theory.} The distribution-\emph{conditioned} $(1+\epsilon)$ bound and the global-optimality of the isolated-obstacle case remain conjectural. \emph{(vi) Deployment.} The methods are validated only in simulation: closed-loop SITL flight and physical trials with wind, sensor noise, and obstacle-detection latency are still required before any operational claim. The contribution is a planning-layer one; the remaining gap is field validation, but we do not claim deployment-readiness.

%% ====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ====================================================================

This paper has shown that corridor-constrained pathfinding is a practical acceleration technique for risk-aware autonomous navigation.
The evidence comes from sixteen experiments covering over 6{,}000 planned paths: risk-annotated grids, procedural port environments, a real container terminal (Penang Port) rasterised from OpenStreetMap data, comparisons against JPS, D*~Lite, and Weighted A*, a corridor width sensitivity sweep, the RILS evaluation, Moving AI Lab benchmarks in uniform-cost, risk-annotated, and maze topologies, a multi-heuristic comparison, 180 progressive discovery missions, an autopilot waypoint-mission feasibility study, and a complexity-bound validation with a verified C++ reference implementation.

On the risk-annotated grids the method was built for, ILS reaches up to $7.90\times$ speedup and cuts node expansions by $86\%$ on gradient risk ($85\%$ on uniform) while staying within $0.2\%$ of optimal for $\lambda \leq 1.0$; hotspot risk is harder, giving smaller but steady $1.7\times$ to $2.4\times$ speedups against an exposure cost we measure rather than hide. RILS buys the last bit of quality back: by widening the corridor exactly where risk is high it holds suboptimality at or below $0.1\%$ on hotspot risk, and it is the variant to reach for when a near-perfect path matters more than the clock.

The value of the method, though, is as much in where it stops working as in where it shines, and the evaluation maps that boundary deliberately. ILS speedup grows with grid size on the port maps ($1.0\times$ to $3.9\times$), and AILS only pays for its adaptive corridor above a grid-size threshold that the large-grid runs pin down. The advantage carries onto a real container terminal (Penang Port) rasterised from OpenStreetMap, where AILS reaches $3.44\times$ over A* at $\lambda = 1.0$ with a $70\%$ node-count reduction. It does not carry everywhere. The full Dragon Age corpus and the maze benchmarks show that on room-and-corridor and labyrinth topologies the corridor loses even with risk weighting, because the optimal path simply does not stay near the start--goal line. The Moving AI runs sharpen the same point from the other side: at $\lambda = 0$ ILS does nothing useful ($0.31\times$ on game maps, $0.99\times$ on random maps), but laying a risk field over the same random maps switches the advantage on ($2.5\times$ to $3.2\times$ at $\lambda = 1.0$). Two conditions, then, not one: a risk-weighted cost \emph{and} a topology that keeps the optimum close to the reference line.

Against the obvious competitors the method sits between heuristic inflation and incremental repair. Weighted A* is far faster ($23\times$ to $56\times$) but pays $2\%$ to $5\%$ in path quality; D*~Lite expands far fewer nodes ($47\%$ to $68\%$ reduction) but collapses on sustained clustered re-planning, where its success rate falls to $17\%$--$42\%$ while corridor search holds mission success above A*'s (up to $73.3\%$ against $60.0\%$). Against JPS the surprise is that ILS matches or beats it in like-for-like pure-Python wall-clock on uniform-cost grids once density reaches $20\%$ (a compiled re-match is left open), and unlike JPS it runs on any cost function at all. Across the four heuristics of Experiment~11 the speedup barely moves, which says the gain comes from the spatial restriction and not from a lucky heuristic choice.

The theory (Sections~\ref{subsec:formal}--\ref{subsec:jps_boundary}) gives the empirical picture a spine: cast as shortest-path over a nested chain of induced subgraphs, corridor search admits proofs of admissibility, termination, containment, completeness, a per-query sub-optimality certificate, a corridor-size bound with a $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ node-expansion reduction, time and space complexity, the unsoundness of Jump Point Search once vertices carry weight, and a proof that no instance-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ guarantee can exist on weighted grids. Experiment~14 then puts those predictions on the bench: the size bound holds without a single violation up to $2000\times2000$, the $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ reduction stays flat across an order of magnitude in grid size, and a C++ reference reproduces the Python node counts (up to tie ordering) while a compiled re-planning benchmark confirms the ILS/AILS wall-clock advantage and the D*~Lite wall-clock loss at production speed, leaving only a compiled JPS comparison open.

What is left is mostly engineering and reach. A compiled implementation should pull the AILS and D*~Lite crossovers down to smaller grids; the autopilot study should grow from waypoint generation into closed-loop SITL flight and then onto real hardware with wind and sensor noise; standardised risk-annotated benchmarks would make cross-paper comparison honest; and a hybrid of RILS with D*~Lite-style incremental repair is the natural next planner for risk fields that change as the vehicle flies.
