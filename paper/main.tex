\title{Risk-Aware Corridor-Constrained Pathfinding for UAV Navigation in Biosecurity-Sensitive Environments}

\author[usm]{Amr Elshahed\corref{cor1}}
\ead{amr.elshahed@student.usm.my}

\author[usm]{Majid Khan Bin Majahar Ali}
\author[usm]{Farah Aini Binti Abdullah}

\address[usm]{School of Computer Sciences, Universiti Sains Malaysia, 11800 Penang, Malaysia}

\cortext[cor1]{Corresponding author.}

\begin{abstract}
Unmanned aerial vehicles operating over container terminals and similar transport facilities need routes that are cheap to recompute yet respect spatially varying operational risk, but classical grid planners scale poorly once a risk term enters the cost function and obstacles change between queries. We study corridor-constrained search, which restricts A* to a spatial band around the start--goal line and widens it only when no path fits. Three variants are evaluated on risk-annotated grids: Incremental Line Search (ILS, fixed width, prior work) and two introduced here that adapt the width---Adaptive ILS (AILS) to local obstacle density and Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS) to the local risk field. Edge cost is Euclidean distance plus a weighted risk term. We cast corridor search as shortest-path over a nested chain of induced subgraphs and prove base-heuristic admissibility (with a conditional guarantee for the optional risk-inflated heuristic), termination, completeness, a per-query sub-optimality certificate, corridor-size and time/space bounds, the unsoundness of Jump Point Search once vertices carry weight, and the non-existence of any risk-weight-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ guarantee on weighted grids. Across sixteen experiments and more than $6{,}000$ planned paths---procedural grids, a real container terminal (Penang Port) rasterised from OpenStreetMap, Moving AI Lab benchmarks, and comparisons with Jump Point Search, D*~Lite and Weighted A*---ILS attains up to $7.89\times$ speedup on gradient risk---a favourable fixed-corner best case, falling to $\approx 3$--$4\times$ at random endpoints---cutting node expansions by up to $86\%$ while holding path cost within $0.2\%$ of optimal on gradient and uniform risk and within $1.3\%$ on hotspot risk for $\lambda \leq 1.0$; AILS reaches $3.26\times$ on the Penang terminal, and RILS holds near-exact optimality. The $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ node reduction holds invariant from $200\times200$ to $2000\times2000$ grids. The result is a parameter-light acceleration layer with a clear applicability envelope rather than a universal speedup.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% ====================================================================

Autonomous unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) now fly biosecurity-sensitive missions: contamination surveillance over port facilities, agricultural pest monitoring, quarantine-zone inspection~\citep{WHO2021,FAO2023}. Two demands pull against each other in these settings. The planner has to be fast enough to re-plan in real time, and it has to reason about spatially varying risk, because a route through a contamination gradient, across a quarantine boundary, or into an active inspection area can be operationally unacceptable even when it is the shortest one on the map.

Classical grid planners (A*, Dijkstra) return optimal paths but with state-space growth quadratic in the grid dimension, and risk weighting worsens it: with cost $\mathrm{cost} = \mathrm{dist} + \lambda \cdot \mathrm{risk}(cell)$ for a user-chosen $\lambda$, the optimal path is pulled off the straight line and the search fans out further than on a plain occupancy grid.

Standard accelerators each solve part of the problem. Jump Point Search~\citep{Harabor2011} prunes symmetric expansions for order-of-magnitude gains, but only on uniform-cost grids; hierarchical schemes (HPA*~\citep{Botea2004}, Contraction Hierarchies~\citep{Geisberger2008}) need expensive preprocessing that assumes a static map; and incremental planners (D*~Lite~\citep{Koenig2002}, LPA*~\citep{Koenig2004}) repair a stale tree cheaply for a few changed cells but bog down on large clustered changes --- exactly what a newly flagged contamination zone or redrawn quarantine boundary looks like. None covers fast re-planning, risk weighting, and clustered-change tolerance at once.

This paper studies corridor-constrained pathfinding as a way to close that gap.
The core idea, formalised as Incremental Line Search (ILS)~\citep{Elshahed2025ILS} and extended here with the density-adaptive variant Adaptive ILS (AILS) and the risk-responsive variant Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS), restricts the search space to a spatial corridor centred on the Bresenham line between start and goal and expands the corridor incrementally only when the initial search fails to find a path.
Prior ILS work showed up to $87.31\%$ execution-time reduction on standard occupancy grids~\citep{Elshahed2025ILS}; we present AILS and RILS in self-contained form (Section~\ref{sec:method}) and evaluate all three on risk-weighted grids, a setting not previously investigated, where the corridor's interaction with the risk landscape yields qualitatively different optimality and efficiency tradeoffs than the uniform-cost case.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The contributions of this paper are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
\item We evaluate ILS on risk-annotated grids across three risk distributions, six risk-weight values, and three obstacle densities. ILS reaches speedups of up to $7.89\times$ with $86\%$ node reduction, and path quality stays within $0.2\%$ of optimal for gradient and uniform risk at $\lambda \leq 1.0$.

\item We introduce Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS), a new variant that adapts corridor width to the local risk level. RILS attains the best path quality among the three corridor methods (under $0.1\%$ suboptimal on hotspot risk, against $0.7$ to $1.2\%$ for ILS and AILS at $\lambda \leq 1.0$) but its speedup is lower. The result clarifies the tradeoff between risk-adaptive corridor construction and search-space restriction, and RILS is therefore the preferred variant when path quality dominates the cost function.

\item We provide a graph-theoretic analysis of corridor-restricted search as optimisation over nested induced subgraphs. It establishes base-heuristic admissibility (with a conditional result for the optional risk-inflated heuristic), termination, containment and completeness, a per-query a-posteriori sub-optimality certificate (a cheap $O(1)$ bound and an exact bound costing one unrestricted search), a corridor-size bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (4r+1)(D_\infty+2r+1)$ with a $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ node-expansion reduction, and time/space complexity for ILS/AILS/RILS. We prove that no risk-weight-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ bound exists for corridor-restricted search on weighted grids, and that Jump Point Search is unsound on vertex-weighted grids, which formally delimits where symmetry pruning and corridor restriction each apply.

\item We compare ILS and AILS against D*~Lite~\citep{Koenig2002} and Weighted A*~\citep{Pohl1970} and show under which operating conditions each method is preferable.

\item On procedurally generated container-port grids with structured obstacle layouts and biosecurity risk layers, ILS scales from $1.1\times$ to $4.1\times$ speedup with grid size. AILS shows a threshold effect, in that its adaptive-corridor overhead exceeds the benefits on grids below $500 \times 500$.

\item In a progressive obstacle discovery simulation over 180 sustained UAV missions, corridor-constrained re-planning achieves higher mission success rates than standard A* (up to $73.3\%$ against $60.0\%$) and $12.6\%$ cumulative node reduction. The cost is somewhat higher path suboptimality.

\item We validate the methods on a real container terminal (Penang North Butterworth Container Terminal) rasterised from public OpenStreetMap data, demonstrating that the speedup--quality tradeoff transfers from procedurally generated grids to a real-world port topology, and providing a publicly reproducible benchmark.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is organised as follows: Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work; Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the ILS, AILS, and RILS algorithms with formal sub-optimality analysis; Section~\ref{sec:experiments} describes the experimental design; Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the results; and Sections~\ref{sec:discussion} and~\ref{sec:conclusion} discuss implications and conclude.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Classical Grid-Based Pathfinding}

Dijkstra's algorithm~\citep{Dijkstra1959} finds shortest paths but explores the entire reachable space; A*~\citep{Hart1968} adds admissible-heuristic guidance for optimality with less exploration, and landmark-based bidirectional ALT~\citep{Goldberg2005} cuts average query time via precomputed landmark distances and the triangle inequality. These form the baseline for acceleration techniques.

\subsection{Symmetry-Breaking and Jump Point Search}

Jump Point Search~\citep{Harabor2011} prunes symmetric expansions on uniform-cost grids; JPS+~\citep{Harabor2014} precomputes jump-point maps, and subgoal graphs~\citep{Uras2013} and rectangular symmetry reduction~\citep{Harabor2014RSR} exploit symmetry differently. All assume uniform cost: their symmetry rules require identical cell costs and break down on risk-annotated grids where costs vary spatially (Lemma~\ref{lem:jps_unsound}).

\subsection{Hierarchical and Preprocessing-Based Methods}

HPA*~\citep{Botea2004} precomputes inter-cluster paths and Contraction Hierarchies~\citep{Geisberger2008} add shortcut edges for near-constant queries on road networks; navigation meshes~\citep{Tozour2002} and Theta*~\citep{Nash2007} support any-angle paths via convex polygons and line-of-sight checks. All require stable environments\,---\,dynamic obstacle changes invalidate the preprocessing and force costly updates.

\subsection{Incremental and Dynamic Re-planning}

D*~Lite~\citep{Koenig2002} and LPA*~\citep{Koenig2004} incrementally repair the search tree when the map changes, and Field D*~\citep{Ferguson2005} adds continuous-cost interpolation. They excel at small, frequent perturbations but degrade when changes are large and clustered, since queue maintenance propagates updates through much of the search space.

\subsection{Risk-Aware and Exposure-Sensitive Planning}

Risk-aware pathfinding adds non-distance costs to the objective. Composite costs $\mathrm{cost} = \mathrm{dist} + \lambda \cdot \mathrm{risk}$ appear in military route planning~\citep{Rowe1990}, hazmat transport~\citep{Erkut1998}, and UAV missions~\citep{Aggarwal2020}, rooted in the continuous weighted-region problem of \citet{Mitchell1991}. Recent UAV work makes the risk field explicit: \citet{Primatesta2019} use a riskA* over a population-risk map, \citet{Tang2023} minimise an urban third-party risk field via min-cost A*, \citet{Karlsson2023} give the incremental D*+ for heterogeneous platforms, and learning-accelerated risk-field A* has been explored~\citep{Xiang2024}. All reshape the cost landscape yet search the full grid, potentially routing optimal paths far from the geometric shortest path and raising cost; the corridor mechanism here is orthogonal and could wrap any of them. It is complementary to bounded-suboptimal (Section~\ref{subsec:bounded}) and sampling/learning-based (Sections~\ref{subsec:sampling}, \ref{subsec:uav_learning}) methods: confining search to a spatial band around the expected path direction limits the extra nodes risk weighting forces into consideration.

\subsection{Bounded-Suboptimal Search}\label{subsec:bounded}

Bounded-suboptimal methods relax optimality for speed. Weighted A*~\citep{Pohl1970} inflates the heuristic by $w>1$ ($f = g + w \cdot h$), guaranteeing $w$-bounded solutions with large speedup; Anytime Weighted A*~\citep{Hansen2007} repeats this with decreasing weights for progressively better solutions; Potential Search~\citep{Stern2014} prunes via a potential function under a user-specified cost bound; and A*$_\epsilon$~\citep{Pearl1982} expands only a FOCAL sublist within a $(1+\epsilon)$ factor of the minimum $f$. These differ from corridor methods in mechanism: they inflate the evaluation function, whereas corridor methods restrict the search space spatially. Heuristic inflation can degrade quality in risk-sensitive settings where cost inflation is unacceptable, while spatial restriction preserves optimality within the constrained region.

\subsection{Sampling-Based and Multi-Objective Planning}\label{subsec:sampling}

Sampling-based methods avoid grid discretization by sampling the configuration space into roadmaps or trees. RRT*~\citep{Karaman2011} reaches asymptotic optimality via cost-based rewiring and handles complex costs in continuous spaces, and PRM* variants~\citep{Karaman2011} extend probabilistic roadmaps to multi-query planning with optimality guarantees; both lack discrete-grid completeness and need heavy computation to converge in high dimensions. For multiple competing objectives, NAMOA*~\citep{Mandow2010} computes the Pareto-optimal path set (e.g., distance vs.\ risk vs.\ time) at added overhead. Such methods suit unstructured or continuous environments; for the structured grids of UAV port operations, classical acceleration via symmetry exploitation and spatial restriction is more efficient without sacrificing optimality.

\subsection{Recent UAV Path Planning and Learning-Based Approaches}\label{subsec:uav_learning}

Learning-based UAV planning has grown recently: deep reinforcement learning maps observations to actions and may discover novel strategies~\citep{Jones2023}, and hybrid classical-plus-ML approaches (learned heuristics or DRL configuration selection) adapt well to dynamic environments~\citep{Jones2023,Aggarwal2020}. For biosecurity-critical operations, however, these require environment-specific training data, lack formal optimality or safety guarantees, and cannot easily encode hard constraints like ``avoid pathogen release zones''. Classical grid methods with explicit acceleration remain preferred where solution quality, completeness, and verifiability are required.

\subsection{Corridor and Geometric Search-Space Restriction}\label{subsec:geometric_restriction}

Restricting search by geometry rather than by an evaluation-function bound is a recurring idea, and we position this work within it. \emph{Preprocessing-based geometric pruning} stores per-edge regions of optimally reachable goals and prunes expansions falling outside: geometric containers~\citep{Wagner2005} and arc-flags for road networks, and the grid-specific goal bounding-box pruning of \citet{Rabin2016}, which pairs per-edge boxes with JPS+ for state-of-the-art uniform-cost queries\,---\,but all require all-pairs preprocessing on a static graph, so obstacle changes invalidate it. \emph{Frontier and cost pruning}\,---\,beam search~\citep{Bisiani1987} (fixed frontier) and bounded-cost / potential search~\citep{Stern2014} (cost bound)\,---\,restrict search without a spatial region. \emph{Trajectory corridors} like the safe flight corridors of \citet{Liu2017} build a convex free-space region \emph{around an already-found path} for trajectory optimisation\,---\,an output container for smoothing, not an input restriction on graph search. Closest in mechanism is the corridor-constrained Hybrid~A* of \citet{Che2026}, which confines a kinodynamic search to a Voronoi-derived corridor and reports an $83.7\%$ reduction in node expansions, but on car-like, uniform-cost motion, with the corridor built from a reference path and no incremental widening or completeness guarantee.

Against this backdrop, \citet{Elshahed2025ILS} formalised Incremental Line Search (ILS), which builds a corridor along the Bresenham start-goal line\,---\,requiring \emph{no preprocessing}\,---\,and widens it on failure, evaluated on uniform-cost occupancy grids. Our contribution is not the corridor idea in the abstract but a specific, defensible combination: (i) a preprocessing-free, incrementally-widened line corridor on \emph{risk-weighted} (vertex-weighted) grids, where the symmetry-based pruning of \citet{Rabin2016,Harabor2011} is unsound (Lemma~\ref{lem:jps_unsound}); (ii) a \emph{risk-field-adaptive} corridor width (RILS) computed in $O(1)$ per reference cell via summed-area tables; and (iii) a formal package\,---\,induced-subgraph framing, containment/termination/completeness, a per-query sub-optimality certificate, corridor-size and complexity bounds, and a proof that no risk-weight-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ bound exists on weighted grids\,---\,absent from prior corridor work. We also present the density-adaptive variant AILS, using integral images for $O(1)$ local density queries (Section~\ref{sec:method}), and the risk-responsive RILS (Section~\ref{subsec:rils}); all three are validated on risk-weighted grids in biosecurity-specific environments.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

We consider pathfinding on a 2D grid graph $G = (V, E, w)$ where $V$ is the set of traversable cells, $E$ connects 8-adjacent free cells, and $w: E \to \mathbb{R}^+$ assigns edge weights.
Each cell $v \in V$ has an optional risk value $\rho(v) \in [0, 1]$; we write $\rho_{\max} = \max_{v \in V} \rho(v) \leq 1$.
The cost of traversing an edge $(u, v) \in E$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cost}
    w(u, v) = d(u, v) + \lambda \cdot \rho(v)
\end{equation}
where $d(u, v)$ is the Euclidean movement cost ($1.0$ for cardinal, $\sqrt{2}$ for diagonal moves) and $\lambda \geq 0$ is the risk-weight parameter.
At $\lambda = 0$ the problem reduces to standard shortest-path planning; increasing $\lambda$ progressively penalises high-risk cells.

The objective is to find a path $\pi^* = (v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_k)$ from start $s = v_0$ to goal $g = v_k$ that minimises:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:objective}
    C(\pi) = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} w(v_i, v_{i+1})
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Corridor as an induced subgraph.}
Let $\ell(s,g)$ be the Bresenham digital segment between $s$ and $g$ (a fixed tie-breaking convention gives one cell per step along the dominant axis, an $8$-connected lattice path of $\|s-g\|_\infty + 1$ cells that is monotone and $1$-Lipschitz in the minor coordinate). For half-width $r \geq 0$ the corridor vertex set is $\mathcal{C}(r) = \{v \in V : d_\infty(v, \ell(s,g)) \leq r\}$, where $d_\infty$ is the Chebyshev distance to the line set, and $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)} := G[\mathcal{C}(r)]$ is the subgraph of $G$ \emph{induced} by $\mathcal{C}(r)$ (an edge is retained iff both endpoints lie in $\mathcal{C}(r)$). With $r_k = r_0 + (k-1)\Delta r$ the induced subgraphs are nested, $G[\mathcal{C}(r_1)] \subseteq G[\mathcal{C}(r_2)] \subseteq \cdots \subseteq G$, and ILS runs an optimal base search on the first member that admits an $s$-to-$g$ path. AILS and RILS replace the constant $r$ by a per-reference-cell radius $r(p)$, yielding a variable-width induced subgraph. This induced-subgraph (vertex-set) restriction is the unifying object of the analysis in Section~\ref{subsec:formal}; it is what distinguishes corridor methods from heuristic-inflation (which alters the evaluation function) and from symmetry pruning (which is sound only on uniform-cost grids, Lemma~\ref{lem:jps_unsound}).

\subsection{Incremental Line Search (ILS)}

ILS~\citep{Elshahed2025ILS} constrains A* search to a spatial corridor $\mathcal{C}$ centred on the Bresenham line $\ell(s, g)$ between start and goal.
The corridor is constructed as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:corridor}
    \mathcal{C}(r) = \{v \in V : \min_{p \in \ell(s,g)} \|v - p\|_\infty \leq r\}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the corridor half-width.
A* search is then restricted to $\mathcal{C}$: only cells within the corridor are expanded.
If the search fails (no path exists within $\mathcal{C}$), the corridor width is increased by $\Delta r$ and the search is re-run.
This expansion continues for up to $K_{\max}$ attempts.

The initial corridor \emph{width} --- its full transverse extent $2r_0$ --- is set as a fraction $\alpha_0$ of the grid diagonal, so the half-width entering $\mathcal{C}(r_0)$ is $r_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\alpha_0 \sqrt{H^2 + W^2}$, where $H$ and $W$ are the grid dimensions. Throughout, $\alpha$ denotes this width fraction, exactly as used by Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils} and reported in the experiments.
Empirically, $\alpha_0 = 0.05$ (a corridor width of $5\%$ of the diagonal, i.e.\ half-width $r_0 = 2.5\%$ of the diagonal) provides a good balance between corridor tightness and path feasibility.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{ILS: Incremental Line Search on Risk-Weighted Grid}
\label{alg:ils}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Grid $G$, start $s$, goal $g$, risk weight $\lambda$, initial width $r_0$, max attempts $K_{\max}$
\ENSURE Path $\pi^*$ or FAILURE
\STATE $r \leftarrow r_0$
\FOR{$k = 1$ \TO $K_{\max}$}
    \STATE Build corridor $\mathcal{C}(r)$ via Bresenham line $\ell(s, g)$
    \STATE $\pi \leftarrow$ A*$(G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}, s, g, \lambda)$ \COMMENT{Search restricted to $\mathcal{C}$}
    \IF{$\pi \neq$ NULL}
        \RETURN $\pi$
    \ENDIF
    \STATE $r \leftarrow r + \Delta r$ \COMMENT{Expand corridor}
\ENDFOR
\RETURN FAILURE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Adaptive ILS (AILS)}

AILS replaces the uniform-width corridor of ILS with a variable-width corridor whose radius at each reference-line cell adapts to local obstacle density.
The key mechanism is the integral image (summed area table) $I$, which enables $O(1)$ density queries:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density}
    \delta(p, \omega) = \frac{1}{(2\omega+1)^2} \sum_{(i,j) \in W(p,\omega)} \mathbf{1}[\text{obstacle at } (i,j)]
\end{equation}
where $W(p, \omega)$ is the $(2\omega+1) \times (2\omega+1)$ window centred at point $p$ on the reference line.

The corridor radius at each reference point is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adaptive_radius}
    r(p) = r_{\min} + (r_{\max} - r_{\min}) \cdot \delta(p, \omega)^{\gamma}
\end{equation}
where $r_{\min}$ and $r_{\max}$ bound the corridor width, and $\gamma$ (the AILS density exponent, distinct from the corridor fraction $\alpha$) controls sensitivity to density changes.
High local density produces a wider corridor (to accommodate detours around obstacles); low density produces a narrow corridor (to maximise search-space exclusion). AILS otherwise follows the shared build--search--widen loop of Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils} (Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart}), replacing the constant radius $r$ with the per-cell radius $r(p)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:adaptive_radius} computed from the obstacle integral image.

\subsection{Risk-Aware Heuristic Adaptation}

On risk-weighted grids the standard octile distance heuristic $h(n, g)$ stays admissible for \emph{every} $\lambda \geq 0$: it lower-bounds the distance term and ignores the non-negative risk term, so it never overestimates true cost. It does, however, become a looser guide as $\lambda$ grows. This admissible $h_0 = h$ is the heuristic used in all of the headline experiments (Experiments~1--12). One may optionally tighten the guidance by inflating it with the local mean-risk factor:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heuristic}
    h_\lambda(n, g) = h(n, g) \cdot (1 + \lambda \cdot \bar{\rho}_0)
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\rho}_0$ is a conservative scalar estimate of mean risk along the remaining path ($\bar{\rho}_0 = 0.3$ based on empirical grid statistics when this optional heuristic is enabled). This constant $\bar{\rho}_0$ is distinct from the windowed local mean $\bar{\rho}(p,\omega)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:risk_query} and the per-path mean $\bar{\rho}_\pi$ introduced below.
This inflation strengthens search guidance, but it is admissible only under the condition made precise in Proposition~\ref{prop:admissible}; its empirical cost excess is bounded in the Experiment~14 audit. The headline experiments therefore use the unconditionally admissible $h_0$.

\subsection{Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS)}\label{subsec:rils}

AILS adapts corridor width based on local obstacle density. Its corridor can therefore be too narrow in regions with sparse obstacles but elevated risk, which are exactly the regions where a wider corridor is needed to allow risk-avoidance detours.
We propose Risk-Responsive ILS (RILS), a variant that adapts corridor width to the local risk distribution rather than to obstacle density.

RILS constructs the corridor along the same Bresenham reference line as ILS and AILS, but determines the radius at each reference point $p$ using the local mean risk within a query window:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:risk_query}
    \bar{\rho}(p, \omega) = \frac{1}{(2\omega+1)^2} \sum_{(i,j) \in W(p,\omega)} \rho(i,j)
\end{equation}
where $W(p, \omega)$ is the $(2\omega+1) \times (2\omega+1)$ window centred at $p$ and $\rho(i,j)$ is the risk value at cell $(i,j)$.
This query is computed in $O(1)$ time using an integral image (summed area table) over the risk layer, analogous to AILS's density queries but operating on the continuous risk values rather than binary obstacle indicators.

The corridor radius at each reference point is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rils_radius}
    r_{\text{RILS}}(p) = r_{\text{base}} + (r_{\max} - r_{\text{base}}) \cdot \bar{\rho}(p, \omega)^\beta
\end{equation}
where $r_{\text{base}}$ is the minimum corridor width (the RILS counterpart of AILS's $r_{\min}$), $r_{\max}$ is the maximum width, and $\beta \geq 0$ controls risk-sensitivity.
In high-risk regions ($\bar{\rho} \to 1$), the corridor approaches $r_{\max}$, providing ample space for the search to find risk-avoidance detours.
In low-risk regions ($\bar{\rho} \to 0$), the corridor narrows toward $r_{\text{base}}$, maximising search-space pruning where risk avoidance is unnecessary.
When no risk layer is present ($\lambda = 0$), RILS degenerates to standard ILS.

AILS and RILS differ only in the adaptation signal used to set the corridor radius. AILS uses $r(p) \propto \delta(p)^\gamma$, where $\delta$ is the local obstacle density, so the corridor widens near obstacles to allow detours around blocked cells. RILS uses $r(p) \propto \bar{\rho}(p)^\beta$, where $\bar{\rho}$ is the local mean risk, so the corridor widens in high-risk regions to allow risk-avoidance routing.

RILS is therefore designed for risk-weighted grids in which the cost landscape, rather than the obstacle layout, determines path optimality.
Consider an open, obstacle-free region with high risk: RILS produces a wide corridor (good for finding risk-avoiding paths), whereas AILS produces a narrow corridor (because obstacle density is zero) and its adaptation signal is misleading.

Default parameters used in experiments: $r_{\text{base}} = 0.05 \cdot \text{diag}$, $r_{\max} = 0.15 \cdot \text{diag}$, $\beta = 1.0$, $\omega = 5$, up to 10 expansion attempts. Like AILS, RILS reuses the ILS loop of Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils} with the per-cell radius of Eq.~\eqref{eq:rils_radius} in place of the constant $r$, the only change being that the integral image is built over the risk layer rather than the obstacle grid.

\subsection{Formal Sub-Optimality Analysis}\label{subsec:formal}
\emph{Proofs of all results in this and the following two subsections are provided in the Supplementary Material.}

We now establish theoretical properties of corridor-constrained search on risk-weighted grids.

\paragraph{Unconditionally admissible heuristic.}
The simplest admissible heuristic for risk-weighted grids uses $\bar{\rho}_0 = 0$, reducing to the standard octile distance:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h0}
    h_0(n, g) = h(n, g)
\end{equation}
Since $h(n,g)$ lower-bounds the obstacle-respecting shortest travel distance $d_{\min}(n,g)$, which is in turn at most the cost $C(\pi)$ of any $n$-to-$g$ path $\pi$ (the added risk terms are non-negative), the heuristic is admissible for all $\lambda \geq 0$ and all risk distributions, unconditionally.
However, $h_0$ ignores risk and provides weak guidance when $\lambda > 0$.

\paragraph{Risk-adjusted heuristic (conditional).}
The optional risk-adjusted heuristic of Eq.~\eqref{eq:heuristic} can strengthen search guidance when $\lambda > 0$; it is one of the heuristics compared in Experiment~11 and its admissibility margin is audited in Experiment~14, but it is \emph{not} the default for the headline experiments, which use $h_0$. We restate it here with explicit conditions:

\begin{proposition}[Conditional Heuristic Admissibility]\label{prop:admissible}
Let $h_\lambda(n, g) = h(n, g) \cdot (1 + \lambda \cdot \bar{\rho}_0)$ with $\bar{\rho}_0 \geq 0$, where $h(n,g)$ is the octile distance on an 8-connected grid. Then $h_\lambda$ is admissible at $n$ provided every path $\pi$ from $n$ to $g$ with $k$ edges and mean risk $\bar{\rho}_\pi$ satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adm_condition}
    k \cdot \bar{\rho}_\pi \;\geq\; h(n,g) \cdot \bar{\rho}_0.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{When the condition holds.}
Condition~(\ref{eq:adm_condition}) is satisfied whenever $k \geq h(n,g)$ and $\bar{\rho}_\pi \geq \bar{\rho}_0$. The first inequality holds for all paths whose minimum-cost step is the cardinal step of length 1 (then $D(\pi) = k$ and $D(\pi) \geq h$ give $k \geq h$); it can fail on pure-diagonal motion, for which $D(\pi) = k\sqrt{2}$ and therefore $k = D(\pi)/\sqrt{2}$ can be below $h(n,g)$.
On such diagonal-dominant paths the condition tightens to $\bar{\rho}_\pi \geq (h/k)\,\bar{\rho}_0$, which in the all-diagonal extreme equals $\sqrt{2}\,\bar{\rho}_0$. When the condition fails, $h_\lambda$ is simply inadmissible at $n$ and A* loses its optimality guarantee.

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
The condition~(\ref{eq:adm_condition}) is sufficient but not necessary for admissibility, and with $\bar{\rho}_0 = 0.3$ it is in fact \emph{frequently violated} even on smooth risk: the audit in Experiment~14 (Table~\ref{tab:exp14_rho}) finds that at $\bar{\rho}_0 = 0.3$, $\lambda = 1.0$, the returned cost would exceed the true optimum on essentially every query for gradient and hotspot risk (and on $94\%$ of uniform-risk queries). What makes $\bar{\rho}_0 = 0.3$ usable as an accelerator is not that it preserves admissibility but that the resulting \emph{excess} is small and grows predictably with $\bar{\rho}_0$ and with the spikiness of the risk field: at $\bar{\rho}_0 = 0.3$ the maximum cost excess is $1.0\%$ on gradient, $2.8\%$ on uniform, and $6.4\%$ on hotspot risk, and at $\bar{\rho}_0 = 0.15$ uniform risk ($\rho \equiv 0.3$) is still genuinely admissible ($0\%$ violations), matching the per-edge bound $\bar{\rho}_0 \leq \rho$. This audit, however, characterises the \emph{optional} $h_\lambda$ accelerator. The headline experiments use the admissible $h_0$, so their A* baseline is exactly optimal and the ILS path-quality figures reflect corridor containment \emph{alone}: Experiment~1 paths stayed within $0.2\%$ of optimal on gradient and uniform risk and $1.3\%$ on hotspot risk for $\lambda \leq 1.0$ (growing to $11\%$ at $\lambda = 5.0$), the residual gap arising only where the optimal path leaves the corridor, not from any heuristic inadmissibility. Enabling $h_\lambda$ instead would add the bounded excess above in exchange for stronger search guidance; $h_0$ is what the headline results use and what keeps the base search exactly optimal ($\bar{\rho}_0 = 0$, zero violations on all distributions in Table~\ref{tab:exp14_rho}).

\begin{proposition}[Corridor Containment and Completeness]\label{prop:complete}
If the optimal path $\pi^*$ lies entirely within corridor $\mathcal{C}(r)$, i.e., $\pi^* \subseteq \mathcal{C}(r)$, then corridor-restricted A* returns $\pi^*$.
Furthermore, the incremental expansion mechanism (up to $K_{\max}$ attempts) guarantees that if a path exists within a corridor of width $r_0 + (K_{\max}-1) \cdot \Delta r$, it will be found.
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Termination.}
The incremental expansion loop terminates after finitely many iterations.

\begin{proposition}[Termination]\label{prop:termination}
For any finite $H \times W$ grid $G$ with start $s$, goal $g$, initial half-width $r_0 \geq 0$, increment $\Delta r \geq 1$, attempt cap $K_{\max}$, and covering width $r_{\mathrm{cover}} = \max(H, W) - 1$ (distinct from the AILS/RILS width cap $r_{\max}$), Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils} (and likewise the AILS and RILS variants) halts in at most $\min\!\big(K_{\max},\, \lceil (r_{\mathrm{cover}} - r_0)/\Delta r \rceil + 1\big)$ iterations and returns the cost-optimal path within the terminal corridor $\mathcal{C}\big(r_0 + (K_{\max}-1)\Delta r\big)$, or FAILURE if that corridor contains no $s$-to-$g$ path. When the cap is large enough to cover the grid, i.e.\ $K_{\max} \geq \lceil (r_{\mathrm{cover}} - r_0)/\Delta r \rceil + 1$, the terminal corridor equals $G$ and the procedure is \emph{complete}: it returns an $s$-to-$g$ path iff one exists in $G$. With a smaller cap --- the experiments use $K_{\max} = 10$ --- completeness is relative to the terminal corridor; unconditional completeness is recovered by raising $K_{\max}$ or by running the final attempt unrestricted.
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{When the global optimum is returned.}
Proposition~\ref{prop:complete} guarantees that the returned path is cost-optimal \emph{within the realised corridor}. The global optimum is returned exactly when that corridor contains a globally optimal path, and this conditional property is what the empirical \emph{path cost ratio} measures.

\begin{proposition}[A-posteriori sub-optimality certificate]\label{prop:certificate}
Let the base algorithm be cost-optimal on any finite weighted graph (A* with an admissible, consistent heuristic, or Dijkstra), let $\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}$ be the returned path, and let $\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}$ be the corridor at the returning attempt. Then $\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}$ is globally optimal on $G$ if and only if $\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}$ contains a globally optimal path; in particular this holds whenever $r^{\dagger} = r_{\mathrm{cover}}$ (then $G|_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} = G$). The excess cost equals the in-corridor detour cost $\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} := C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}) - C(\pi^*) \geq 0$, and admits two computable certificates:
\begin{align}
\text{(loose)}\quad 0 \;\leq\; \Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} &\;\leq\; C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}) - \underline{C}(s,g), \label{eq:cert_loose}\\
\text{(exact)}\quad \Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} &\;=\; C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}}) - C(\pi^*), \label{eq:cert_exact}
\end{align}
where $\underline{C}(s,g)$ is any admissible lower bound on $C(\pi^*)$ (e.g.\ the octile relaxation, evaluable in $O(1)$). The loose certificate~\eqref{eq:cert_loose} costs $O(1)$ beyond the search already performed; the exact certificate~\eqref{eq:cert_exact} requires one unrestricted shortest-path computation on $G$, i.e.\ $O(|V|\log|V|)$ additional time.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textit{Remark (heuristic consistency).}
The optimality guarantees above require a base search that is optimal on the weighted graph, i.e.\ A* with an admissible \emph{and consistent} heuristic, or Dijkstra. The risk-adjusted heuristic $h_\lambda = h\,(1+\lambda\bar{\rho}_0)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:heuristic} scales the octile distance by a constant $\kappa = 1+\lambda\bar{\rho}_0 \geq 1$; using consistency of $h$, a sufficient condition for $h_\lambda$ to be consistent is $(\kappa-1)\,d(u,v) \leq \lambda\rho(v)$ on every edge, i.e.\ $\bar{\rho}_0\,d(u,v) \leq \rho(v)$, which fails in general. The certificates and optimality statements consequently attach to the unconditionally admissible variant $h_0 = h$ (or to Dijkstra) --- the heuristic used throughout Experiments~1--12, so the reported A* costs are exact optima and ILS sub-optimality is attributable to corridor containment alone; with the optional $h_\lambda$ they hold up to the conditional-admissibility margin of Proposition~\ref{prop:admissible}.

\begin{corollary}[Additive Sub-Optimality Bound]\label{cor:bound}
Let $\pi_r^*$ be the optimal path within $\mathcal{C}(r)$ and $\pi^*$ the globally optimal path, and let the returning corridor be $\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}$. The additive detour is bounded by the in-corridor path length times the maximum edge weight,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:additive_bound}
\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} \;=\; C(\pi_r^*) - C(\pi^*) \;\leq\; \ell_{\max}(\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}) \cdot w_{\max}(G),
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{\max}(\mathcal{C}^{\dagger})$ is the maximum number of edges on an in-corridor $s$-to-$g$ path and $w_{\max}(G) = \max_{(u,v) \in E} w(u,v) = \sqrt{2} + \lambda\rho_{\max}$ is the maximum edge weight. Dividing by $C(\pi^*) > 0$ gives the multiplicative form $C(\pi_r^*)/C(\pi^*) \leq 1 + \ell_{\max}(\mathcal{C}^{\dagger})\,w_{\max}(G)/C(\pi^*)$. This is a worst-case envelope (it discards the $-C(\pi^*)$ term); the operationally useful per-query bound is the a-posteriori certificate of Proposition~\ref{prop:certificate}, of which this is the distribution-agnostic relaxation.
Empirically, the normalised detour $\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}}/C(\pi^*)$ stayed below $0.002$ for gradient and uniform risk at $\lambda \leq 1.0$ (Table~\ref{tab:exp1_speedup}) and below $0.013$ for hotspot risk at $\lambda \leq 1.0$, far inside this worst-case envelope.
\end{corollary}

The bound establishes that corridor-constrained search is near-optimal when the corridor captures the globally optimal path or a close substitute.
The incremental expansion mechanism serves as a safety net: if the initial corridor is too narrow, successive expansions widen it until a feasible path is found or the attempt cap $K_{\max}$ is reached (Proposition~\ref{prop:termination}).
The empirical path quality results in Section~\ref{sec:results} confirm that this worst-case bound is loose; actual sub-optimality is substantially smaller.

\paragraph{On tighter bounds.}
The bound~\eqref{eq:additive_bound} is distribution-agnostic and therefore necessarily loose.
Distribution-specific behaviour differs sharply. For uniform risk ($\rho = c$), $\Delta_{\mathcal{C}^{\dagger}} = 0$ trivially since all paths incur identical risk per unit distance.
For gradient risk (where $\rho$ varies smoothly), the detour is governed by the local risk-gradient magnitude $\|\nabla\rho\|_{\max}$ and the corridor width, so the cost slack shrinks with a smoother field; deriving a tight constant here is the open conditional-bound question stated below.
Hotspot risk admits no simple tighter bound, because the deviation depends on whether the optimal path's detour around a hotspot falls inside or outside the corridor, which is a configuration-dependent property.

The natural question is whether a corridor method can guarantee a \emph{risk-weight-independent} multiplicative bound, that is, a single constant $(1+\epsilon)$ that holds for every weighted grid irrespective of the risk weight $\lambda$. It cannot: no such bound exists for corridor-restricted search on risk-weighted grids (for any fixed $\lambda$ the ratio is at most $1+\lambda$, but no constant dominates the whole family).

\begin{theorem}[No risk-weight-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ bound]\label{thm:no_bound}
For every $\epsilon > 0$ and every initial half-width $r_0 \geq 1$ there exists a risk-weighted $8$-connected grid (the model of Section~\ref{sec:method}) and a start--goal pair on which the incremental corridor procedure (Algorithm~\ref{alg:ils}) terminates at half-width $r^{\dagger} = r_0$ and returns a path whose cost ratio $C(\pi_{\mathrm{ILS}})/C(\pi^*)$ exceeds $1 + \epsilon$.
\end{theorem}

The theorem resolves, in the negative, whether a distribution-agnostic tight bound is attainable; it also explains why the empirical safety net is the incremental expansion mechanism rather than an a-priori ratio guarantee. The a-posteriori certificate of Proposition~\ref{prop:certificate} is the constructive counterpart: although no uniform bound exists, each individual query carries a computable bound on its own sub-optimality.
Deriving tight $(1+\epsilon)$ bounds \emph{conditioned} on specific smooth risk distributions (e.g.\ bounded-gradient fields) remains open.

\subsection{Complexity of Corridor-Restricted Search}\label{subsec:complexity}

We now quantify the search-space saving and the running time of the corridor methods. Let $D_\infty = \|s - g\|_\infty$ and let $r \geq 0$ be an integer half-width.

\begin{theorem}[Corridor-size bound]\label{thm:size}
Counting all grid cells, $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (4r+1)(D_\infty + 2r + 1)$. If the reference line is axis-aligned the sharper bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (2r+1)(D_\infty + 2r + 1)$ holds.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Node-expansion reduction]\label{cor:reduction}
Dijkstra (or A* with an admissible, consistent heuristic) on $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}$ expands at most $|\mathcal{C}(r)|$ vertices in $O(|\mathcal{C}(r)| \log |\mathcal{C}(r)|)$ time. If the corridor contains a globally optimal path (no widening), this is $O(r D_\infty + r^2)$ expansions. On a square $N \times N$ grid with $D_\infty = \Theta(N)$ and half-width $r = \alpha N/\sqrt{2}$ (the corridor width fraction $\alpha$ above, since $2r = \alpha\sqrt{H^2+W^2}$) one has $|\mathcal{C}(r)| = \Theta(\alpha N^2)$ (the bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:size} is matched from below: the band contains at least $(2r+1)$ cells in each of $\Theta(N)$ columns), so restricted search expands $\Theta(\alpha N^2)$ vertices, a $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ reduction relative to the $\Theta(N^2)$ worst case of unrestricted search.
\end{corollary}

\begin{theorem}[Complexity of ILS, AILS, RILS]\label{thm:complexity}
Let $N_{\!*} = \max(H,W)$ and let binary-heap Dijkstra, or A* with a consistent heuristic, cost $T_{\mathcal{A}}(n) = O(n \log n)$ on $n$ vertices of a bounded-degree grid. Then: \textup{(i)} the Bresenham line costs $O(N_{\!*})$ and the AILS/RILS summed-area table costs $O(HW)$ time and space with $O(1)$ windowed queries; \textup{(ii)} iteration $k$ costs $O(HW) + T_{\mathcal{A}}(|\mathcal{C}(r_k)|)$ (dense mask), or $O(|\mathcal{C}(r_k)|)$ with a corridor-local mask; \textup{(iii)} the loop runs at most $\min(K_{\max}, \lceil (r_{\mathrm{cover}}-r_0)/\Delta r \rceil + 1) = O(\min(K_{\max}, N_{\!*}/\Delta r))$ iterations, where $r_{\mathrm{cover}} = N_{\!*} - 1$ guarantees $\mathcal{C}(r_{\mathrm{cover}}) \supseteq V$; \textup{(iv)} worst case the time is $O(N_{\!*} \cdot HW \log(HW))$, i.e.\ $O(N^3 \log N)$ on a square grid, with $O(HW) = O(N^2)$ space; the favourable single-corridor case is $O(HW)$ preprocessing plus $O(|\mathcal{C}(r_0)| \log |\mathcal{C}(r_0)|)$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Geometric Containment Conditions}\label{subsec:containment}

For an $s$-to-$g$ path $\pi$ let $\zeta(\pi) = \max_{v \in \pi} d_\infty(v, \ell(s,g))$, and let $\zeta^\star = \min\{\zeta(\pi^*): \pi^* \text{ globally optimal}\}$.

\begin{lemma}[Containment]\label{lem:containment}
If $r \geq \zeta^\star$ then some globally optimal path lies in $\mathcal{C}(r)$, and optimal base search on $G|_{\mathcal{C}(r)}$ returns a globally optimal path.
\end{lemma}

\begin{corollary}[Free-line optimality]\label{cor:freeline}
Under the corner-cutting movement convention of Section~\ref{sec:experiments}, if $\lambda = 0$ and the Bresenham line $\ell(s,g)$ is obstacle-free, then $\zeta^\star = 0$ and ILS returns a global optimum at $r = 0$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proposition}[Isolated $d_\infty$-ball obstacle: feasibility]\label{prop:isolated_feas}
Let $\lambda = 0$ and let the obstacle set lie in a single Chebyshev ball $B = \{v: d_\infty(v,p_0) \leq b\}$ centred at a reference cell $p_0 \in \ell(s,g)$, with $s,g \notin B$ and the free region $\{v: d_\infty(v,\ell) \leq b+1\}\setminus B$ $8$-connected and containing $s,g$. Then a feasible $s$-to-$g$ path of deviation $\leq b+1$ exists, so ILS terminates with a solution at $r = b+1$ that is cost-optimal within $\mathcal{C}(b+1)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{conjecture}[Isolated convex obstacle: global optimality]\label{cj:convex}
Under the hypotheses of Proposition~\ref{prop:isolated_feas}, $\zeta^\star \leq b+1$, so ILS returns a \emph{global} optimum at $r = b+1$. A full proof needs an octile-metric exchange argument re-routing any optimal detour onto the ring without increasing cost; we have verified this numerically on all tested isolated-ball instances but do not yet have a general proof.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{conjecture}[Bounded-gradient deviation]\label{cj:gradient}
On an obstacle-free grid with $\Gamma$-Lipschitz risk ($|\rho(u)-\rho(v)| \leq \Gamma d_\infty(u,v)$), the least-deviating optimal path satisfies $\zeta^\star \leq f(\lambda,\Gamma)$ for some $f$ independent of grid size; by Lemma~\ref{lem:containment} the corridor cost slack is then exactly $0$ for all $r \geq f(\lambda,\Gamma)$. The constant $f$ is open; the uniform case $\Gamma = 0$ gives $f = 0$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:freeline}).
\end{conjecture}

\subsection{Soundness Boundary: Symmetry Pruning on Weighted Grids}\label{subsec:jps_boundary}

The corridor mechanism restricts the \emph{vertex set} and therefore preserves in-corridor optimality for any cost function (Lemma~\ref{lem:containment}). Symmetry-pruning methods such as JPS instead prune \emph{moves} under a uniform-cost assumption, and that assumption is essential.

\begin{lemma}[JPS is unsound under vertex weights]\label{lem:jps_unsound}
There is a vertex-weighted $8$-connected grid on which Jump Point Search, using the canonical pruning rules of \citet{Harabor2011}, returns a path of cost strictly greater than the optimum.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}
JPS optimality on uniform-cost grids rests on a symmetry lemma: every optimal path has a same-\emph{length} canonical representative through jump points. Under vertex weights equal length no longer implies equal cost, so the canonical representative ($P_1$) can be costlier than the optimum ($P_2$, $P_3$). This is why the present work restricts JPS to $\lambda = 0$ and adopts vertex-set (corridor) restriction as the weighted-grid mechanism. The result also bounds what a JPS-inside-corridor hybrid can claim: such a hybrid is sound only on the uniform-cost portions of the cost landscape.
\end{remark}

\section{Experimental Design}\label{sec:experiments}
%% ====================================================================

We carried out sixteen experiments (1--14, with 10b, 10c benchmark variants), plus two D*~Lite runs (4b, 5b) reported with Experiments~4 and~5 in the Discussion. Experiments~1--5 form the core evaluation; 6--9 cover additional baselines, sensitivity analysis, and RILS; 10, 10b, 10c validate on Moving AI Lab benchmarks (including risk-annotated and maze topologies); 11 evaluates heuristic sensitivity; 12 validates on a real container terminal (Penang Port, Malaysia) rasterised from OpenStreetMap, addressing external-validity concerns about the procedural grids of Experiments~1--11; 13 tests autopilot-mission feasibility via ArduPilot-compatible waypoint missions; and 14 validates the complexity theory (corridor-size bound and $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ reduction) on grids up to $2000 \times 2000$ and establishes C++/Python parity. Supplementary 4b and 5b compare D*~Lite against ILS on clustered re-planning and progressive missions.
The headline experiments (1--12) use 8-connected movement with the octile heuristic $h_0$, which is admissible on the risk-weighted graph because risk enters only the edge cost. The A* baseline is thus optimal and ILS returns the optimal path \emph{within its corridor}, so any reported ILS sub-optimality reflects corridor containment alone. The risk-adjusted heuristic $h_\lambda$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:heuristic} is an optional accelerator (compared in Experiment~11, audited in Experiment~14), not used for headline results.
Diagonal moves cost $\sqrt{2}$ and permit \emph{corner-cutting} (a diagonal step is admissible whenever its destination cell is free). This model is identical for A*, ILS, AILS, RILS and JPS in every experiment, so all reported speedups, node reductions, and within-experiment cost ratios compare like with like, and the C++ reference of Experiment~14 matches it. The sole exception is the full Dragon Age corpus comparison of Experiment~10, scored against the published \texttt{.scen} optima and therefore \emph{disabling} corner-cutting to match the convention under which those optima were computed---consistently on both sides.
As node-count and runtime distributions are right-skewed, significance uses the Wilcoxon signed-rank test on paired samples with Holm correction across each experiment's configurations; effect sizes are matched-pairs rank-biserial correlations, and CIs are $95\%$ percentile bootstrap ($10{,}000$ resamples). The threshold is the Holm-corrected $\alpha = 0.05$, which all significant node-count differences below survive. All experiments ran on an Apple MacBook Pro (M1~Pro, 16~GB RAM), so wall-clock comparisons are within-experiment on identical hardware; the implementation-independent node-count metric is comparable throughout.

\paragraph{Baseline scope.}
Baselines span one acceleration \emph{category} each: heuristic inflation (Weighted A*), incremental repair (D*~Lite), and symmetry pruning (JPS), versus the corridor's spatial restriction. Three methods are out of scope. Goal bounding-box pruning~\citep{Rabin2016} and geometric containers~\citep{Wagner2005} are the closest spatial-restriction competitors but require all-edges preprocessing of a \emph{static} graph, so they do not apply to the dynamic re-planning experiments (4, 4b, 5, 5b) motivating the corridor's preprocessing-free design; on static experiments they are a fair, open baseline. Theta*~\citep{Nash2007} and Field~D*~\citep{Ferguson2005} solve the any-angle and continuous-cost problems, a different class from the 8-connected discrete-cost setting here.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Risk-Annotated Grids}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We generated $200 \times 200$ binary occupancy grids at three densities (10\%, 20\%, 30\%), each overlaid with one of three risk distributions: \emph{gradient} (linear top-left to bottom-right), \emph{hotspot} (Gaussian-centred high-risk zones), and \emph{uniform} (constant). Six risk weights were tested:
\[
\lambda \in \{0,\; 0.1,\; 0.5,\; 1.0,\; 2.0,\; 5.0\}.
\]
Each density--risk--$\lambda$ configuration used 100 random maps (5,400 paths total), with start $(0,0)$ and goal $(199, 199)$.

\paragraph{Algorithms.}
A* (baseline) versus ILS~A* (5\% initial corridor, up to 10 expansion attempts). Metrics: runtime (ms), nodes expanded, path cost ratio (ILS/A*), cumulative risk exposure ratio ($\sum_{v \in \pi}\rho(v)$ relative to A*), and corridor expansion count.

\paragraph{Statistical protocol.}
Node-count differences at each configuration follow the shared protocol (Cohen's $d$ additionally reported for continuity with the prior ILS study).

\subsection{Experiment 2: Port Environment Validation}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Procedural container-port grids reproduce real port structure: a quay zone (sparse), a container yard (rectangular blocks with narrow aisles), a gate zone (barriers with controlled openings), and a buffer zone (mixed clutter), with biosecurity risk elevated near container blocks and gate checkpoints. Three grid sizes ($200 \times 200$, $300 \times 300$, $500 \times 500$) and three risk weights ($\lambda \in \{0, 0.5, 1.0\}$) were tested, with 30 start--goal pairs per configuration (270 total).

\paragraph{Port generation parameters.}
Zone proportions (relative to grid height): quay $0$--$15\%$, buffer $15$--$20\%$, container yard $20$--$70\%$, gate $75$--$85\%$, open $85$--$100\%$. \paragraph{Setup.}
This experiment validates Sections~\ref{subsec:complexity}--\ref{subsec:jps_boundary} and settles the implementation confound, in two parts. \emph{(a) Bound and scaling:} on procedural $N \times N$ risk grids ($N \in \{200, 300, 500, 750, 1000, 1500, 2000\}$) we verify the corridor-size bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (4r+1)(D_\infty+2r+1)$ on every (reference-line, $r$) case, and measure the node-expansion reduction $\text{nodes(A*)}/\text{nodes(ILS)}$ and space ratio $|V|/|\mathcal{C}|$ versus corridor fraction $\alpha$ and grid size, testing the $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ prediction of Corollary~\ref{cor:reduction}. \emph{(b) Implementation parity:} we re-implemented A*, ILS, AILS, and a canonical JPS in C++17 with a shared instance loader, verifying on 3{,}000 paired A*-family instances ($256 \times 256$, mixed density and risk) that the C++ and Python references return identical path cost and node-expansion counts (up to equal-$f$ tie ordering). \subsubsection{Node Reduction}

Node reduction followed the same $\lambda$ pattern: $0$--$31\%$ at $\lambda = 0$ (pure corridor effect), $41$--$84\%$ at $\lambda = 1.0$ (gradient highest at $83$--$84\%$, hotspot lowest at $41$--$51\%$), peaking at $86.1\%$ for gradient at $\lambda \geq 2.0$ (fewer than 1 in 7 nodes vs.\ A*). Reductions were significant under the Holm-corrected Wilcoxon test (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}) at $\lambda \geq 0.5$ and at $\lambda = 0.1$ for all but the $10\%$-density hotspot case ($0.07\%$), but not for the near-zero $\lambda = 0$ sparse grids. We report the node-reduction \emph{ratio} with bootstrap CIs rather than parametric effect sizes, as node counts are near-deterministic across repeats.

\subsubsection{Path Quality and Risk Exposure}

Path quality depended strongly on both risk distribution and $\lambda$:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Gradient risk:} Path cost ratio at most $1.001$ across all $\lambda$ values (suboptimality below $0.1\%$). The smooth risk landscape meant the corridor consistently captured near-optimal paths.
\item \textbf{Uniform risk:} Ratio at most $1.001$ for all configurations, that is, effectively optimal, consistent with the spatially invariant nature of uniform risk.
\item \textbf{Hotspot risk:} Ratio at most $1.013$ for $\lambda \leq 1.0$, degrading to $1.11$ at $\lambda = 5.0$ for 30\% density. The localised nature of hotspots created detours that occasionally fell outside the corridor.
\end{itemize}

Risk exposure matched A* for gradient and uniform risk ($\leq 0.1\%$), but for hotspot risk at high $\lambda$ it exceeded A* by up to $9.1\times$ (at $\lambda = 5.0$, 30\% density), as the tight corridor could not route around distant hotspots. This exposure--speed tradeoff is tunable: the penalty stays below $2.5\times$ for $\lambda \leq 1.0$ but grows sharply at extreme weights.

\paragraph{Robustness to endpoint geometry.}
The fixed corner-to-corner endpoints used above favour a diagonal corridor, so we re-ran the same protocol with \emph{random} start--goal pairs (minimum separation $40\%$ of the diagonal; $100$ random maps per cell across the $18$ density--risk--$\lambda$ cells, i.e.\ $1{,}800$ nominal instances with $64$--$96$ solved per cell). At $\lambda = 1.0$ (nine cells), three things change and one does not. The corridor advantage persists (ILS $3.22\times$ mean speedup, $58.5\%$ node reduction; AILS $4.04\times$, $64.9\%$), with mean cost ratios of $1.004$ (ILS) and $1.006$ (AILS); near-optimality holds \emph{on average} rather than uniformly, as the densest hotspot cells reach $1.011$ (ILS) and $1.018$ (AILS). So the advantage is not an artefact of the corner geometry. But the \emph{ranking flips}: with random endpoints AILS overtakes ILS ($4.04\times$ against $3.22\times$), because the diverse path topologies let its density-adaptive width pay off, whereas with fixed corners ILS leads (Experiments~1--5). And the \emph{magnitude} is lower: the $7.89\times$ peak is specific to the corner geometry; representative random-endpoint speedups are $3$ to $4\times$. We therefore report the corner-to-corner figures as a favourable best case and the random-endpoint figures as the representative operating point, and we recommend AILS over ILS whenever path requests are diverse.

Contrary to expectation, RILS did not outperform ILS or AILS in speedup or node reduction anywhere: AILS was fastest ($2.2\times$ to $8.8\times$), then ILS ($1.8\times$ to $6.5\times$), then RILS ($1.0\times$ to $1.8\times$). The cause is RILS's wider corridors, which let A* expand more nodes: on gradient risk, non-zero mean risk everywhere makes its corridor $1.5$ to $2$ times wider than ILS's, while on hotspot risk it widens only near hotspots, giving modest overall speedup ($1.0\times$).

RILS does deliver the best path quality of the three on every distribution: cost ratio $\leq 1.001$ on gradient and hotspot risk (vs.\ $1.008$ ILS, $1.013$ AILS on hotspot at $\lambda = 1.0$), with the hotspot gap widening at $\lambda = 2.0$ ($1.003$ vs.\ $1.027$ ILS, $1.042$ AILS). RILS is thus a path-quality-oriented variant for biosecurity scenarios intolerant of risk-exposure deviation, with two caveats: its edge over ILS is third-decimal except on hotspot risk (where its speedup falls to $\sim 1.1\times$), and the open, obstacle-free, high-risk scenario that motivated it (Section~\ref{subsec:rils}) is not isolated by any experiment here. It is best read as a conservative quality option whose designed-for niche remains to be demonstrated, not a speed contribution.

Unexpectedly, AILS overtook ILS under random endpoints ($8.8\times$ vs.\ $6.5\times$ on gradient risk), reversing the Experiments~1--5 ranking, because its density-adaptive corridor better handles the varying-density regions that random straight-line references cross.

Table~\ref{tab:exp12_penang} tells the same story as the synthetic experiments, in three respects.

\emph{First, the risk-weighted activation pattern transfers to real data.}
At $\lambda = 0$ corridor methods give little benefit (ILS $0.97\times$, RILS $0.97\times$, $\approx 4\%$ reduction; AILS only $1.56\times$). \emph{Second, AILS dominates speedup on the real port.}
On the $500 \times 500$ Penang grid AILS reached $3.26\times$ at $\lambda = 1.0$ with $70.3\%$ node reduction, consistent with Experiment~9 (mean $5.2\times$, $67.1\%$ on synthetic $500 \times 500$) and Experiment~2 ($1.81\times$ procedural port). At $\lambda = 0.5$ and $1.0$ RILS gave cost ratios $1.0000$ and $1.0002$, against $1.005$--$1.011$ (ILS) and $1.010$--$1.018$ (AILS), at the cost of a speedup near $1.0\times$ --- matching Experiment~8's finding that RILS is the quality-oriented variant. JPS is inapplicable at $\lambda > 0$, so the comparison there falls to the corridor methods.

Experiment~12 thus carries the mechanism onto a real OSM-rasterised port: the AILS speed and RILS quality advantages both survive, and risk-weighted activation is a property of the cost landscape, not of how grids are generated. The grid is reproducible from the released bounding box and rasterisation procedure.

\subsection{Experiment 14: Complexity-Bound Validation and Parity Results}\label{subsec:exp14_results}

\emph{Corridor-size bound.} Across $336$ (reference-line, $r$) cases spanning $N \in \{200,\dots,2000\}$, the bound $|\mathcal{C}(r)| \leq (4r+1)(D_\infty+2r+1)$ held with \emph{zero violations}; the tightest case reached $98.6\%$ of the bound, confirming both its validity and that it is not loose.

\emph{$\Theta(1/\alpha)$ node-expansion reduction.} Table~\ref{tab:exp14_scaling} and Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} report the node-reduction factor at four corridor fractions across grid sizes. The reduction is essentially \emph{invariant to $N$} from $200$ to $2000$ at each $\alpha$ (e.g.\ $6.0$--$6.4\times$ at $\alpha=0.05$ across all sizes), while it scales as $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ across $\alpha$ ($1.7\times \to 3.2\times \to 6.2\times \to 15\times$ as $\alpha$ halves from $0.20$ to $0.02$). The measured reduction tracks the space ratio $|V|/|\mathcal{C}| \approx 0.38/\alpha$ to within a few percent. For the diagonal corridor used here (half-width $r = \alpha N/\sqrt{2}$, so the band area is $|\mathcal{C}| \approx 4rN = 2\sqrt{2}\,\alpha N^2$), Corollary~\ref{cor:reduction} predicts $|V|/|\mathcal{C}| \approx 1/(2\sqrt{2}\,\alpha) \approx 0.35/\alpha$, matching the empirical $0.38/\alpha$ up to the boundary-term residual --- the quantitative confirmation that the reduction is a property of the corridor fraction, not of grid size.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Positioning}

Corridor methods sit between heuristic inflation and incremental repair. They beat JPS in pure-Python wall-clock on uniform-cost grids at density $\geq 20\%$ (compiled re-match open) and run on cost functions JPS cannot touch; they keep mission success at $67$--$70\%$ where D*~Lite collapses to $17$--$42\%$ under clustered change; and they trade a little speed (vs.\ wA*'s $22$--$56\times$) for an order-of-magnitude better path quality ($0.3\%$ vs.\ $2$--$5\%$). RILS is the quality specialist; AILS overtakes ILS once endpoints are random (Experiment~8, already at $200\times200$), while grid size separately governs AILS's \emph{absolute} speedup, where its adaptive width pays for its integral-image overhead. The corridor is an acceleration \emph{layer}: any optimal base search slots inside it, and on low-density uniform grids JPS itself could serve as that base.

\subsection{Practical recommendations}\label{subsec:practical}

For routine surveillance, use ILS with $\lambda \in [0.5, 1.0]$ and $\alpha = 0.05$ ($3$--$7\times$ speedup, $\leq 0.3\%$ suboptimal). Switch to AILS when path requests have diverse (random) endpoints, where its density-adaptive width pays off ($5.2\times$ vs.\ $3.5\times$ on large synthetic grids, $3.26\times$ on the Penang port); with fixed or corner-like endpoints ILS's simpler corridor leads (e.g.\ $4.14\times$ vs.\ AILS's $1.81\times$ on the $500\times500$ procedural port of Experiment~2) and avoids the overhead. When risk exposure tolerance is near zero, use RILS ($\leq 0.1\%$ suboptimal). For clustered re-planning, prefer ILS/AILS over D*~Lite; for time-critical work that tolerates $2$--$5\%$ suboptimality, Weighted A* with $w = 1.5$ ($7$--$41\times$) is the better tool.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several threats to validity accompany the results. \emph{(i) Implementation.} All timings are pure-Python, and the overhead caveat cuts both ways: where competitors win on node count but lose on wall-clock (D*~Lite, JPS) we attribute the loss to constant-factor overhead, so the corridor methods' own wall-clock wins carry the same caveat. Experiment~14 addresses this --- a released C++ reference reproduces the Python node counts on $3{,}000$ A*-family instances, and the compiled benchmark (Table~\ref{tab:exp14_cpp}) confirms the ILS/AILS advantage ($5.9$--$7.5\times$ over A*) and the D*~Lite loss both persist at compiled speed. The one comparison not re-run compiled is JPS on the static uniform-cost task, so the ``matches/beats JPS'' observation remains pure-Python evidence pending that measurement; JPS node counts are moreover not directly comparable to A*-family expansions (Section~\ref{sec:results}). \emph{(ii) Risk realism.} Risk fields are synthetic everywhere, including the ``real'' ports, whose layers are assigned by rule from OSM polygon semantics rather than measured; Experiment~12 validates transfer to real \emph{topology}, not real risk, and biosecurity is a motivating application, not a validated capability. \emph{(iii) Endpoint geometry.} Experiments~1--5 use fixed corner-to-corner endpoints favouring a diagonal corridor, on which ILS leads; with random endpoints (Experiments~6--8) AILS overtakes, so best-case figures (e.g.\ the $7.89\times$) are tied to corner geometry and the variant ranking is endpoint-dependent. \emph{(iv) Heuristic.} Headline experiments use the unconditionally admissible $h_0$, so the A* baseline is exactly optimal and reported ILS sub-optimality is corridor containment, not heuristic error; the \emph{optional} $h_\lambda$ accelerator is only conditionally admissible and frequently inadmissible at $\bar{\rho}_0=0.3$ (Section~\ref{subsec:formal}, Table~\ref{tab:exp14_rho}), so enabling it would turn the optimality claims into near-optimality. \emph{(v) Theory.} The distribution-\emph{conditioned} $(1+\epsilon)$ bound and the global-optimality of the isolated-obstacle case remain conjectural. \emph{(vi) Deployment.} The methods are validated only in simulation: closed-loop SITL flight and physical trials with wind, sensor noise, and obstacle-detection latency are required before any operational claim. The contribution is planning-layer; the remaining gap is field validation, and we do not claim deployment-readiness.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ====================================================================

This paper has shown corridor-constrained pathfinding is a practical acceleration technique for risk-aware autonomous navigation. The evidence comes from sixteen experiments over 6{,}000 planned paths: risk-annotated grids, procedural ports, a real container terminal (Penang Port) rasterised from OpenStreetMap, comparisons against JPS, D*~Lite, and Weighted A*, a corridor-width sweep, the RILS evaluation, Moving AI Lab benchmarks (uniform-cost, risk-annotated, maze), a multi-heuristic comparison, 180 progressive discovery missions, an autopilot waypoint-mission feasibility study, and a complexity-bound validation with a verified C++ reference.

On the risk-annotated grids it was built for, ILS reaches up to $7.89\times$ speedup and cuts node expansions by $86\%$ on gradient risk ($85\%$ on uniform) while staying within $0.2\%$ of optimal for $\lambda \leq 1.0$ (up to $1.3\%$ on hotspot); hotspot risk is harder, giving steady $1.6\times$ to $2.5\times$ speedups against an exposure cost we measure rather than hide. RILS buys back the last quality: by widening the corridor where risk is high it holds suboptimality $\leq 0.1\%$ on hotspot risk, the variant for when a near-perfect path matters more than the clock.

The method's value is as much in where it stops working, and the evaluation maps that boundary deliberately. ILS speedup grows with grid size on the port maps ($1.0\times$ to $3.9\times$), and AILS only pays for its adaptive corridor above a grid-size threshold the large-grid runs pin down. The advantage carries onto the real Penang Port terminal, where AILS reaches $3.26\times$ at $\lambda = 1.0$ with $70\%$ node reduction --- but not everywhere. The full Dragon Age corpus and maze benchmarks show the corridor loses even with risk weighting on room-and-corridor and labyrinth topologies, where the optimal path does not stay near the start--goal line. The Moving AI runs sharpen this from the other side: at $\lambda = 0$ ILS does nothing useful ($0.31\times$ on game maps, $0.98\times$ on random), but a risk field over the same random maps switches the advantage on ($2.6\times$ to $3.0\times$ at $\lambda = 1.0$). Two conditions, then: a risk-weighted cost \emph{and} a topology keeping the optimum near the reference line.

Against the obvious competitors the method sits between heuristic inflation and incremental repair. Weighted A* is far faster ($23\times$ to $56\times$) but pays $2\%$ to $5\%$ in quality; D*~Lite expands far fewer nodes ($47\%$ to $68\%$) but collapses on sustained clustered re-planning ($17\%$--$42\%$ success), where corridor search holds mission success above A*'s (up to $73.3\%$ against $60.0\%$). Against JPS, ILS matches or beats it in like-for-like pure-Python wall-clock on uniform-cost grids once density reaches $20\%$ (compiled re-match open), and unlike JPS runs on any cost function. Across Experiment~11's four heuristics the speedup barely moves, so the gain comes from the spatial restriction, not a lucky heuristic.

The theory (Sections~\ref{subsec:formal}--\ref{subsec:jps_boundary}) gives this a spine: cast as shortest-path over a nested chain of induced subgraphs, corridor search admits proofs of admissibility, termination, containment, completeness, a per-query sub-optimality certificate, a corridor-size bound with $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ node-expansion reduction, time and space complexity, the unsoundness of JPS once vertices carry weight, and a proof that no risk-weight-independent $(1+\epsilon)$ guarantee can exist on weighted grids. Experiment~14 confirms these: the size bound holds without violation up to $2000\times2000$, the $\Theta(1/\alpha)$ reduction stays flat across an order of magnitude in grid size, and the C++ reference reproduces the Python node counts (up to tie ordering) while the compiled benchmark confirms the ILS/AILS advantage and D*~Lite loss at production speed, leaving only a compiled JPS comparison open.

What remains is mostly engineering and reach: a compiled implementation should pull the AILS and D*~Lite crossovers down to smaller grids; the autopilot study should grow from waypoint generation into closed-loop SITL flight and then real hardware with wind and sensor noise; standardised risk-annotated benchmarks would make cross-paper comparison honest; and a hybrid of RILS with D*~Lite-style incremental repair is the natural next planner for risk fields that change as the vehicle flies.
